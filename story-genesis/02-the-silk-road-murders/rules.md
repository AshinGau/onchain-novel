# Rules: The Silk Road Murders

## Setting
- **Time**: Present day, spanning approximately one week.
- **Primary locations**: Nong Khai (Thai border town on the Mekong), Bangkok (Charoen Krung Road antiquities district), Nakhon Phanom (riverside night market), and the Lao border region (Houaphan Province, specifically the abandoned village of Ban Nahin).
- **Atmosphere**: Humid, oppressive heat; monsoon-weight air; the Mekong as both highway and graveyard. A landscape where national borders dissolve into irrelevance and smuggling routes centuries old still operate under new management.
- **Social context**: The story sits at the intersection of art crime, human trafficking, and the old overland trade routes (locally called "the Silk Road") that have carried silk, opium, jade, and people across the mountains of mainland Southeast Asia for centuries. Corruption is institutional; protection comes from power, not law.

## Characters
- **Inspector Kittisak Charoenpol**: Royal Thai Police, stationed in Nong Khai -- a four-year exile described as "reassignment." Twenty-three years on the force. Methodical, observant, haunted. He carries a defining guilt: he personally requested that his brother Somchai's death be ruled accidental drowning to protect his own career. His diligence is penance. He underlines things in his notebook when they bother him. Steady hands; everything else shakes.
- **Dr. Apinya**: District medical examiner in Nong Khai. Small, efficient, speaks in clipped sentences. Discovers the silk thread in the ligature wound and the borderland-specific star anise in the stomach contents.
- **Sergeant Prapat**: Kittisak's young sergeant. Still squeamish at crime scenes.
- **Laurent Voss**: Belgian national, age 43, Bangkok-based antiquities dealer. Licensed but suspected of facilitating Khmer artifact exports. Missing for four months before his tortured body appears in the Mekong. All fingernails removed perimortem, garroted with handwoven silk thread. He kept a coded ledger documenting fifteen years of transactions -- every artifact, buyer, and bribed official. He wanted out because the Syndicate started trafficking children. His riddle: "I left it where the silk begins, in the house that remembers."
- **Sasithorn Mongkolsiri**: Laurent's Thai business partner. Art history degree from Chulalongkorn. Mid-thirties, scar on left jawline, nails bitten to the quick. She placed the business card in the dead man's pocket to direct the investigation toward Kittisak specifically, because Laurent trusted him through the connection to Somchai. Pragmatic, controlled, possibly manipulative -- Kittisak cannot determine if she is the most honest or most dangerous person in the case.
- **Somchai Charoenpol**: Kittisak's younger brother, officially drowned twelve years ago near Chiang Khong. Actually a courier on mountain smuggling routes. His final letter mentioned "the silk people" and a warehouse. A photograph received on Kittisak's phone shows Somchai -- twelve years dead -- standing in a doorway in Ban Nahin, metadata dating the image six days ago.
- **The Broker (Khun Prasert)**: Thai national, educated abroad (likely Europe), late fifties, unremarkable in appearance. Runs the Syndicate with cellular organization. Uses different names in every country. Alibi for Laurent's murder: temple ceremony in Chiang Mai, witnessed by two police officers.
- **David Chen**: Taiwanese-American buyer. Charity gala alibi in Singapore, but his private jet filed a suspicious flight plan to Udon Thani the next morning.
- **Madame Nguyen**: Vietnamese textile exporter. Government-meeting alibi in Hanoi, but her shipping routes overlap exactly with Laurent's smuggling corridors.
- **Fon**: Kittisak's contact at the Art Crime division in Bangkok. Discovers the anomaly of Ban Nahin -- abandoned village with satellite-visible lights.

## Story Rules
- **Genre**: Literary crime thriller. The pacing is deliberate and atmospheric, not action-driven. Tension comes from information asymmetry and moral complexity, not from chase scenes.
- **Narrative voice**: Third-person limited, closely following Kittisak. Prose is precise, sensory-rich, and understated. Descriptions of landscape and food carry emotional weight. Metaphors tend toward water, rivers, and things that dissolve.
- **The Mekong as character**: The river is omnipresent -- as transportation corridor, crime scene, metaphor, and almost a sentient force. It "always took the shoes." It carries bodies "like passengers no one claims." Every major scene connects to water.
- **Alibi architecture**: Three suspects, three perfect alibis. The story establishes that perfect alibis are themselves suspicious -- "real alibis have gaps." Future chapters should maintain and complicate this structure rather than simply eliminating suspects.
- **Moral weight**: Kittisak's guilt over Somchai is the emotional engine. Every investigative choice is shadowed by his original sin of choosing career over truth. The case is simultaneously professional and deeply personal.
- **Avoid**: Do not resolve the Somchai photograph too quickly -- it is the story's most powerful hook. Do not simplify the Syndicate into cartoon villainy; it should feel institutional, banal, and terrifyingly competent. Do not let Kittisak become an action hero; his strength is patience and observation. Do not abandon the sensory texture of Southeast Asian settings.

## Plot Direction
- **Ban Nahin**: Kittisak is about to cross into Laos illegally to investigate the abandoned silk-weaving village with satellite-visible lights. This is the most immediate plot thread. He will be operating without jurisdiction, badge, or backup -- becoming what Somchai was.
- **The ledger**: "Where the silk begins, in the house that remembers" -- likely Ban Nahin, the silk-producing village. Finding the ledger would expose the entire Syndicate. It is the story's MacGuffin.
- **Somchai alive?**: The photograph showing Somchai in Ban Nahin six days ago is either genuine (he survived/was held captive for twelve years), fabricated (a lure to draw Kittisak across the border), or something else entirely. This mystery must be handled with care.
- **Sasithorn's true role**: She claims to be an ally, but she also admits to controlling the narrative by choosing which investigator would find the body. Her relationship with Laurent ("not romantic -- as a partner, a friend") and her four months of independent investigation raise questions about what she knows and hasn't shared.
- **The Syndicate's pivot to trafficking children**: This is the moral stakes escalator. Laurent wanted out because of this. The ledger presumably documents it. This thread must be treated with gravity, never exploitatively.
- **The Indochine Trading Co. symbol**: The figure-eight/silkworm cocoon symbol scratched into fresh plaster on Charoen Krung Road. A marker of the Syndicate's presence? Connected to the "Brotherhood" or simply a trade sign?
- **The black sedan**: Someone is watching Kittisak. Likely Syndicate surveillance, but the identity and intent remain open.
- **Kittisak's reckoning**: The story is building toward a moment where Kittisak must choose again -- truth or self-preservation -- but this time with full knowledge of the cost.
