Bootstrap Chapter 2: The Man Who Drowned Twice

Kittisak had a file he kept in a locked drawer at home, not at the station. A manila envelope, soft with age, containing three photographs, a handwritten letter, and a police incident report dated twelve years ago. The report described the recovery of a body from the Mekong near Chiang Khong -- male, late twenties, identified as Somchai Charoenpol, younger brother of then-Sergeant Kittisak Charoenpol.

Cause of death: drowning. Case status: closed.

This was what the file said. What it did not say was that Somchai had been working as a courier on the old mountain routes between Laos and Thailand. What it did not say was that Kittisak had known about his brother's work for six months and done nothing. What it did not say was that the body bore bruises inconsistent with river trauma, and that the investigating officer had closed the case in eleven days.

Eleven days. Kittisak had counted them many times.

He opened the envelope at 2 a.m., sitting on the floor with a glass of Mekhong whiskey. He did not look at the photographs. He looked at the letter -- mailed two weeks before Somchai's death -- which contained a single line that had haunted him for twelve years:

"The silk people are real, brother. I've seen the warehouse."

And now Laurent Voss, an antiquities dealer, had been garroted with handwoven silk thread and deposited in the same river that had carried Somchai's body. Kittisak did not believe in coincidence. He believed in patterns, and this one had the shape of something that had been waiting for him.

---

He drove to Bangkok the next morning -- seven hours by car rather than one by plane, because he did not want his travel in airline databases. His superintendent had reassigned the Voss case to the Bangkok Metropolitan Police. Kittisak's involvement was, technically, over.

Technically.

The shop on Charoen Krung Road was shuttered, a notice reading "temporarily suspended." The coffee shop two doors down was open, and the woman behind the counter watched him with the wary attention of someone who recognized police.

"Sasithorn Mongkolsiri," he said, showing identification. "Laurent Voss's business partner."

"She left. Two, three months ago. Right after the police came the first time, when he went missing."

"Did she seem frightened?"

The woman considered this. "She seemed like someone who had been expecting bad news and finally received it."

Kittisak bought a coffee he didn't want and called Fon, his contact at the Art Crime division.

"Laurent Voss," Fon said. "Suspected of facilitating Khmer artifact exports. We could never build a case -- he had protection."

"What kind?"

"The kind that makes files disappear. I pulled his folder two years ago and half the documents were missing. Someone with access had cleaned it."

While waiting for Fon to check on Sasithorn, Kittisak walked the street. The afternoon heat pressed down like a flatiron. He passed gem shops throwing colored light onto the pavement, a Hindu temple wedged between banks. At a narrow alley, he paused. A faded sign in four languages read: INDOCHINE TRADING CO., EST. 1923. Below it, scratched into fresh plaster, was a small symbol: a figure eight on its side, or perhaps a silkworm's cocoon.

Kittisak photographed it and walked on.

His phone rang. Not Fon. Unknown number.

"Inspector Kittisak." A woman's voice, Thai with a Lao accent. "My name is Sasithorn. Laurent is dead, and the people who killed him know you have his card."

"How do you know about the card?"

"Because I'm the one who put it in his pocket. In a ziplock bag, so it would survive the river. He was already dead when I found him. I couldn't save him, but I could make sure someone found him. Someone specific."

"Why me?"

"Because of your brother."

Kittisak steadied himself against the doorframe.

"Laurent knew Somchai," she continued. "They worked the same routes years ago. Laurent always said your brother was the only honest person he'd met in the trade. He told me: if anything happens, find the policeman in Nong Khai."

"What was Laurent carrying when they took him?"

"A ledger. Handwritten, in a code he devised. Fifteen years of transactions -- every artifact, every buyer, every official who was paid. Names, dates, amounts. He started keeping it five years ago, when he decided he wanted out."

"Why did he want out?"

A long pause. "Because they started moving children."

The street reassembled itself around Kittisak -- heat, exhaust, the smell of grilled pork.

"Where is the ledger now?"

"Laurent hid it before they took him. He said: 'It's where the silk begins.' I've been trying to decode that for four months."

"I need to meet you."

"Not Bangkok. Not Nong Khai -- they watch the crossings. The night market in Nakhon Phanom, Friday. Come alone. If I see anyone with you, you'll never hear from me again."

The line went dead.

Kittisak stood watching the street, cataloging faces. A man on a motorbike passed slowly, glanced at him, circled back. Probably nothing.

He drove home that night. Seven hours through darkness, the highway cutting through rice paddies that gleamed like black glass under the moon. Twice he checked his mirrors and found the same headlights three cars back. The third time, they were gone.

He thought about the thing he had never told anyone -- not his superintendent, not his ex-wife, not the monk he confessed to at the temple near his apartment.

Somchai's death had not been investigated because Kittisak himself had asked for the case to be closed. He had called the investigating officer and requested, in the careful language of professional courtesy, that it be treated as accidental drowning. Somchai's smuggling work would have been exposed, and exposure would have ended Kittisak's career by association.

He had chosen his career over the truth of his brother's death.

This was his secret. It sat in his chest like a stone, and every river body since -- seven, before Laurent Voss -- had added weight. His diligence was penance. Every case he solved was an attempt to pay a debt that could never be settled.

And now the debt had found him. Somchai's ghost, wearing the face of a Belgian art dealer, had washed up on his stretch of river, pointing him toward the people he had spent twelve years refusing to look for.

He arrived home at 1 a.m. and opened the manila envelope again. This time, he looked at the photographs.

Somchai at a temple fair, age twenty-seven. Somchai on the tailgate of a pickup, mountains behind him, cigarette in hand. Somchai's body on the riverbank at Chiang Khong, face down, arms spread.

The same posture as Laurent Voss.

Kittisak set down his whiskey. His hands were steady. They were always steady -- it was everything else that shook.

He dialed Fon's number. "I need everything you have on silk smuggling routes between Laos and Thailand. Going back twenty years."

"Kit, it's one in the morning."

"I know what time it is."

A sigh. "This is about more than Laurent Voss, isn't it?"

"Yes," he said. "It's about more than Laurent Voss."

He hung up and looked at the river. The lights of Laos wavered on the water like a code he was only beginning to read.
