Bootstrap Chapter 1: The River Gives Back

The dead man had no fingernails. That was the first wrong thing. The second was the ziplock bag in his pocket.

Nong Khai's riverside vendors were laying out catfish and morning glory on banana leaves when the Mekong nudged the body onto the mudflat below the customs pier. A boy chasing a loose chicken saw it first -- face down, arms wide, rotating in the brown current as though trying to embrace the river bottom. The boy did not scream. Children along the Mekong learn the river's vocabulary early: flood, drought, and the dead who travel its length like passengers no one claims.

Inspector Kittisak Charoenpol received the call at 6:47 a.m. while eating khao tom at a plastic table outside his apartment, a ceiling fan stirring the humidity above his head. The rice porridge was still too hot to eat, and he remembers this detail because he never got to finish it. Twenty-three years with the Royal Thai Police, the last four exiled to this border town for reasons his personnel file described as "reassignment," and breakfast remained the only ritual the job had not yet corrupted.

The crime scene was already contaminated by the time he arrived. Fishermen had dragged the body fully onto the bank, and a circle of onlookers had gathered -- market women with their arms crossed, a monk in saffron robes holding a black umbrella against the early sun, three Lao boat drivers who had crossed the river to watch. Kittisak noted all of them. In border towns, everyone watches everything, and no one sees anything.

"Male, maybe forty, forty-five," said Dr. Apinya, the district medical examiner, already crouching beside the body. She was a small woman who spoke in clipped, efficient sentences, as though words cost money. "Been in the water two days, maybe three. Hard to say with this heat."

Kittisak squatted next to her. The dead man wore a linen shirt, now river-stained to the color of weak tea, and trousers with a European cut. His shoes were missing -- the Mekong always took the shoes. His skin had the bloated pallor of drowning, but Kittisak had seen enough river dead to notice what was wrong.

"His fingernails," he said.

Dr. Apinya nodded. "All ten. Removed before death, based on the tissue response. Someone took their time with this man."

The face was difficult. Swelling and river damage had erased the features into something generic -- a middle-aged man, possibly Western, possibly Eurasian. Dark hair, cut short. No tattoos, no birthmarks. In his trouser pocket, sealed in a small ziplock bag as though someone wanted it to survive the water, they found a single item: a business card.

The card read: LAURENT VOSS / Antiquities & Fine Art / Bangkok, and below the name, a phone number with a Thai prefix.

Kittisak held the card by its edges. The ziplock bag was deliberate. Someone had wanted this card found.

"Has anyone called Bangkok?" he asked his sergeant, a young man named Prapat who was trying not to look at the fingernails.

"Not yet, sir."

"Don't. Not yet."

He stood and surveyed the scene -- the brown river sliding south, the Lao side dissolving in morning haze, the Friendship Bridge upstream carrying its first trucks into another country. Somewhere between here and the headwaters, someone had tortured a man and placed him in the current like a message in a bottle.

Kittisak photographed the business card before bagging it. Then he walked the mudflat in widening circles, the way he'd been trained decades ago, though the river was not a room and evidence here dissolved with every tide. He found nothing else. The Mekong was efficient with its secrets.

By noon, the heat had turned the air to gauze. Kittisak sat in his office at the Nong Khai police station -- a concrete building whose air conditioning had been "under repair" since the previous administration -- and made phone calls.

Laurent Voss. The name returned results quickly. A Belgian national, age forty-three, resident of Bangkok for eleven years. Licensed antiquities dealer with a shop on Charoen Krung Road. Known to the Art Crime unit in Bangkok, though never charged. His passport showed frequent crossings into Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam -- and one anomaly: a trip to Houaphan Province, Laos, recorded three times in the past year, each visit lasting exactly four days. The kind of man who moved through borders the way water moves through a net.

But here was the first strangeness: Laurent Voss had been reported missing four months ago. The report had been filed by his business partner, a Thai woman named Sasithorn Mongkolsiri. Four months. And the body in the river had been dead perhaps three days.

Where had Laurent Voss been for four months?

Kittisak called Sasithorn's number. It rang six times, then connected to a voice he had not expected -- male, young, speaking English with an accent he couldn't place.

"Who is this?" the voice asked before Kittisak could speak.

"Inspector Kittisak Charoenpol, Royal Thai Police, Nong Khai station. I'm calling about Laurent Voss."

A silence. Then the line went dead.

He called back. The number was no longer in service.

Kittisak wrote this in his notebook, then underlined it twice. He was a man who underlined things when they bothered him, and this bothered him considerably. He looked at the photocopy of the business card pinned to his corkboard.

Someone had wanted the police to find Laurent Voss. Someone had wanted Kittisak specifically -- a border-town inspector with limited jurisdiction and no political connections -- to find Laurent Voss. The ziplock bag was not the act of a careless killer. It was an invitation.

In his experience, invitations from murderers were best declined. But the body was in his jurisdiction, and the paperwork had already been filed.

At 3 p.m., Dr. Apinya called with preliminary results.

"Cause of death was not drowning," she said. "Ligature marks on the neck, consistent with garroting. The fingernails were removed perimortem -- while he was alive, but likely in the final hours. And there's something else."

"Go on."

"I found silk thread embedded in the ligature wound. Not ordinary silk. I've sent a sample to the university in Khon Kaen, but from what I can see under magnification, it's handwoven. Very fine. The kind of silk they make in certain villages in Laos and northern Vietnam."

Silk thread. Kittisak thought of the old caravan routes -- the overland paths that had carried silk, opium, jade, and occasionally people across the mountains for centuries. The locals still called those paths the Silk Road, though they bore no relation to the ancient one. Different geography, same human cargo.

"One more thing," Dr. Apinya added. "The stomach contents. His last meal was pho -- beef pho, specifically. With Thai basil, not the Vietnamese variety. He ate it within six hours of death."

Thai basil in pho. That was wrong -- or was it? Vietnamese pho typically used Thai basil, so the herb itself was not unusual. But Dr. Apinya had found something else: traces of a local variety of star anise, the kind grown only in a narrow corridor along the Vietnamese-Lao border. The pho had been prepared within fifty kilometers of that corridor. A kitchen in the borderlands, where supply lines and national identities blurred into irrelevance.

Kittisak drove to the river that evening. The mudflat where the body had been found was empty now, the market vendors gone home, the spot where Laurent Voss had come to rest already smoothed by the current. He stood at the water's edge and watched the Lao side darken as the sun dropped behind the mountains.

His phone buzzed. An unknown number, a message in English:

"You found him. Now find what he was carrying."

Kittisak stared at the screen. The Mekong moved past in silence, the color of old bronze, carrying its cargo south toward the sea. He saved the message, then turned off his phone -- a habit from his years working narcotics, when turning off the phone was the only way to stop the work from following you into sleep.

But sleep, he knew, was already out of the question.

He drove back through town, past the night market where tourists ate som tam under colored lights, past the temple where monks were chanting the evening prayers, past the Friendship Bridge now lit up like a runway into Laos. At every stop sign, he thought about Laurent Voss -- art dealer, missing person, murder victim -- and about the four months between disappearance and death.

Four months was a long time to keep a man alive. Long enough to transport him across several countries. Long enough to extract whatever information his fingernails had been the price of. Long enough for the people who killed him to decide, with the calm precision of that ziplock bag, exactly where and when the body should be found.

Kittisak parked outside his apartment and sat in the car with the engine off. The heat closed in immediately, thick and wet and smelling of jasmine from the neighbor's garden. He reached for his phone, turned it back on, and read the message again.

"You found him. Now find what he was carrying."

He had been a policeman long enough to know when he was being used. The question was: by whom, and toward what end?

He was about to go inside when he noticed the car. A black sedan, parked fifty meters down the street, engine off, windows tinted. It had not been there when he arrived. As he watched, the headlights flashed once -- a slow, deliberate pulse -- and then the car pulled away and turned the corner without signaling.

Kittisak did not write this in his notebook. Some things were better kept off paper.

The river kept moving. It always did.
