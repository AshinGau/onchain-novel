Chapter 3: The Weight of Alibis

Nakhon Phanom sat on the Mekong like a town that had forgotten to modernize. French colonial buildings lined the riverfront, their shuttered windows watching the water with exhausted elegance. The Friday night market spread along the promenade, and Kittisak arrived early enough to walk it twice.

Sasithorn found him at eight o'clock. She materialized beside him at a noodle stall, ordered boat noodles in unaccented Thai, and sat on the plastic stool next to his without introduction.

Mid-thirties, slender, with a scar along her left jawline she made no effort to hide. Her hands were what he noticed most -- art historian's hands, long-fingered and precise, but the nails were bitten to the quick and there was a callus on her right index finger from gripping a pen for hours. Or a knife. No jewelry. Dark linen pants, flat shoes suitable for running. She sat with her back to the wall and her eyes on the crowd.

"You came alone," she said. Not a question.

"I did."

"Good. I have forty minutes."

"Then talk."

"Laurent was recruited in 2013 by a man he knew only as the Broker. The Broker connects sellers with buyers, arranges transport, pays border officials. The structure is cellular -- each person knows only the links directly above and below. Laurent never met anyone above the Broker."

"And your role?"

"I authenticated pieces. Art history degree from Chulalongkorn. I thought the business was legitimate enough -- many dealers operate in gray areas. By 2017, I understood the scale. Entire temple sites in Cambodia stripped bare. Bronze Buddhas from Luang Prabang that had been in monasteries for centuries."

"And you stayed."

"I knew too much to leave safely. And Laurent was building the ledger -- our insurance policy."

Kittisak filed this. The night market sizzled around them -- grills, vendors, a folk song from a distant speaker. The river smell mixed with charcoal smoke and lemongrass.

"Describe the Broker."

"Thai national. Educated abroad -- Laurent guessed Europe. Late fifties. Uses different names in every country. In Thailand, Khun Prasert. Laurent said he was unremarkable -- average height, average build. The kind of man who disappears in a crowd."

"And the ledger's location. You said Laurent used a riddle."

"His exact words: 'I left it where the silk begins, in the house that remembers.' He loved riddles. Thought they were safer than coordinates."

"Now let's talk about alibis," Kittisak said. "Laurent died around last Sunday. I've made inquiries."

He opened his notebook. "David Chen, Taiwanese-American buyer. Sunday night he was at a charity gala at the Raffles Hotel, Singapore. Two hundred witnesses, timestamped photos. Flawless." He paused. "But his private jet filed a flight plan from Singapore to Udon Thani the following morning -- ninety minutes from here. Listed as a refueling stop. Except Udon Thani has no facility for refueling private jets."

Sasithorn's hand tightened around her chopsticks.

"Madame Nguyen, Vietnamese textile exporter. She was in Hanoi meeting government officials. Schedule documented minute by minute. Also flawless. But her company ships through the same Lao border crossings Laurent used. Same customs brokers. Same freight forwarders."

"And the Broker?"

"Khun Prasert was at a temple ceremony in Chiang Mai. Multiple witnesses, including two police officers he knows personally. Impeccable."

"And me?"

"Last Sunday, I have no information about your whereabouts."

"I was in Sakon Nakhon. Guesthouse called Baan Rim Nam, room seven."

"I'm sure you can provide details."

"I didn't kill Laurent." Her voice was level. "I loved him. Not romantically -- as a partner, a friend. I risked my life to make sure his body was found."

"To control the narrative. To reach the investigating officer first and shape his understanding."

"That's the most cynical interpretation possible."

"Cynicism is a professional qualification." He leaned forward. "Every suspect has a perfect alibi. That's the problem. Real alibis have gaps. When they're all airtight, it means they were planned -- either separately, or together."

She was quiet for a moment. An old man nearby played a khene, the Lao mouth organ, its reedy voice cutting through the noise like wire through silk.

"There's something else," she said. "Three days before Laurent was taken, he received a crate at the shop. The size of a suitcase, marked as textile samples from Houaphan Province, Laos. He opened it after hours. The next morning, he was different. Terrified. He told me to memorize the riddle and told me about your brother. The crate was gone when I arrived."

Houaphan Province. Remote, mountainous, known for silk weaving and the caves where the Pathet Lao based their revolution. Also a major transit point on the old smuggling routes.

Sasithorn stood. "My forty minutes are up."

"One more question. The Syndicate moving children -- do you have evidence?"

"It's in the ledger. Which is why I found you, Inspector." She looked at him with an expression he couldn't fully read. "Laurent said your brother died because he got too close to the truth. I'm giving you the same truth, twelve years later."

She walked into the crowd and vanished -- absorbed by the market as though she had never existed.

Kittisak stared at his notebook. Three perfect alibis. A riddle he couldn't solve. A woman who was either the most honest person in this story or the most dangerous.

His phone buzzed. Fon:

"Kit -- pulled the silk route files. Something strange. A village called Ban Nahin, Houaphan Province. Major silk-producing village until 2011, when the entire population relocated overnight. Government says flood evacuation, but there was no flood that year. Village is listed as abandoned. But satellite imagery from last month shows lights."

Lights in an abandoned village. A crate from Houaphan Province. A ledger hidden where the silk begins.

He walked to his car and unfolded his paper map on the steering wheel. Ban Nahin sat deep in the mountains, accessible by a single road branching off the highway between Sam Neua and Vieng Xai -- the cave city where revolutionaries had hidden for a decade, invisible to the world.

Crossing into Laos meant operating outside his jurisdiction, his authority, the protection of his badge. It meant becoming what Somchai had been: a man moving through the borderlands with no sanction, using the same routes the Syndicate controlled.

Three suspects, three perfect alibis. A dead man with no fingernails. A missing ledger. A brother whose death might finally make sense. And somewhere in the mountains, a village that was supposed to be empty but wasn't.

He started the engine.

His phone buzzed one last time. Unknown number. Not a text -- a photograph. A wooden house on stilts, surrounded by mulberry trees, mountains rising behind it in morning haze. The window frames were painted the deep red of lac dye, the same dye used to color Lao silk for centuries.

And standing in the doorway, half-turned as though caught mid-step, was a figure Kittisak recognized.

Not Laurent Voss. Not Sasithorn. Not the Broker.

Somchai.

His brother. Twelve years dead. Standing in a doorway in a village that should be empty, in a photograph whose metadata -- which Kittisak would check three times before believing -- showed it had been taken six days ago.

The river followed him in the dark. But now the dead were following too.
