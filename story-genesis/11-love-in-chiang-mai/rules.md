# Rules: Love in Chiang Mai

## Setting
Present-day Chiang Mai, Thailand. The story unfolds over approximately twelve days across the old city and surrounding areas. Key locations include: the songthaew (red truck) routes, Wat Phra That Doi Suthep, Wat Chedi Luang, Wat Phan Tao, Wat Chiang Man, Wat Lok Molee, the Three Kings Monument, the old city moat, the night bazaar, Wualai Saturday walking street, Doi Inthanon national park, a Karen hill tribe coffee village, an elephant sanctuary, and the guesthouses on Ratchadamnoen and Moon Muang roads. The atmosphere is sensory and warm -- jasmine, exhaust, incense, frangipani, grilled pork, mango sticky rice. The city itself functions as a character: ancient, patient, indifferent to arrivals and departures.

## Characters
- **Lena** -- American freelance architectural photographer from Portland, Oregon. Late twenties or early thirties. Commissioned by a design magazine to photograph Lanna temple architecture. Left a four-year relationship with David because his vision of a settled domestic life suffocated her. Her mother told her her laugh was "too big for rooms." She has a habit of counting steps and a deep instinct to maintain independence -- she reacts sharply when she feels someone is being proprietary. She photographs the space around people rather than the people themselves, a pattern that reveals her way of seeing: indirectly, through context. She is running from stillness but drawn to it.

- **Callum** -- Scottish, though his accent has been worn down by years abroad. Teaches English at a language school in Seoul. Previously lived in Tokyo. Holds a master's degree in literature he describes as useless. Has a notebook of first sentences for novels he will never write -- six years of them. His father died the previous year while Callum was in Seoul; his mother has not forgiven him for missing the dying. He has a tic of tapping his thumbnail against his teeth when deciding whether to be honest. He reads mostly Japanese fiction in translation. Cannot eat coriander (genetic). Bought a small silver ring at the Wualai walking street and put it on his pinkie without explanation. His life is "designed for leaving."

- **David** (mentioned only) -- Lena's ex-partner in Portland. Kind, stable, certain. Wanted a house and a dog. Represents the life Lena could not inhabit. Five months of mutual silence.

- **Khun Noi** -- Sixty-year-old Thai cooking class instructor near the east gate. Teaches with surgical authority. Tells Callum to be angry at the chili.

## Story Rules
- **Genre**: Literary romance with a melancholic, reflective tone. Not a love story with a happy resolution -- a love story about the impossibility of resolution.
- **Narrative voice**: Third person, close to Lena's perspective primarily, but with moments of shared interiority. The prose is precise, image-driven, and uses long sentences that build through accumulation. Metaphors are grounded in the physical world (architecture, light, geography).
- **Pacing**: Slow and deliberate. Days function as chapters of emotional escalation. The countdown structure (twelve days, nine days, five days, one day) creates inherent tension.
- **Dialogue style**: Economical and layered. Characters say less than they mean. Subtext carries more weight than text. Banter reveals intimacy; silences reveal depth.
- **Central metaphor**: "The light or the thing the light touches" -- the question of whether you can separate observation from connection, art from feeling. This question should recur and evolve.
- **What to avoid**: Sentimentality, melodrama, easy reconciliation. Do not resolve the central tension (two lives designed for leaving). Do not make either character the villain. Do not reduce Chiang Mai to exotic backdrop -- the city must have agency and texture. Avoid cliched travel romance tropes (no airport chase, no last-minute declaration).

## Plot Direction
- Callum has returned to Seoul. Lena has seven more days in Chiang Mai alone. His text message reframes their parting as "an interruption" rather than an ending.
- Lena has not replied to his message. The question of whether she will -- and what she would say -- is the primary open thread.
- Callum's notebook of first sentences is a metaphor for his fear of continuation (commitment, follow-through). Whether he can move past the first sentence -- in writing and in life -- is unresolved.
- Lena's relationship with stillness vs. movement remains in tension. David represented stillness she couldn't breathe inside; Callum represents movement that mirrors her own restlessness. The question is whether she can find a third option.
- The "furusato" theme (longing without a place) connects both characters. Future chapters could explore whether either finds a place, or whether the longing itself becomes home.
- Lena's photography practice (shooting the space around a person, never the person directly) could evolve -- will she eventually photograph Callum directly? What would that mean?
- Callum's grief over his father and estrangement from his mother is unresolved. Seoul is where he hides from Edinburgh. This thread has room to develop.
