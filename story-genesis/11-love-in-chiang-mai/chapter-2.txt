Bootstrap Chapter 2: Cultural Clash and Attraction

The monks receive alms at dawn. Lena knows this because she has set her alarm for five-thirty, which is not something the version of herself that existed six days ago would recognize as a choice she would make. But the city is teaching her a different rhythm, and she wants to see it before she understands it well enough to stop being surprised.

She stands on Ratchadamnoen Road in the gray half-light with her camera and watches the procession. Monks in saffron, barefoot on the cool pavement, their alms bowls held before them like dark planets. Local women kneel along the route, placing rice and fruit into each bowl with a quiet that feels architectural -- not silence but the deliberate construction of a space where noise is unnecessary. The transaction is not gratitude or charity; it is something older, a daily contract between the visible and the invisible, and Lena photographs it with the awareness that her camera is the loudest thing on the street.

She does not photograph faces. She learned this in Guatemala, years ago -- a woman at a market whose expression, when the shutter clicked, was not anger but something worse: the weariness of being seen without being asked. A camera is an argument. It says: I have the right to look. And sometimes you do not.

Callum is already at Wat Chedi Luang when she arrives at seven-thirty. He is sitting on the low wall near the entrance, reading his Murakami, the spine cracked in three places. He taps his thumbnail against his teeth, finishes a sentence, looks up.

"You're exactly on time," he says. "This is becoming a pattern."

The ruined chedi dominates the temple grounds. It rises in a stepped pyramid of weathered brick, its upper half collapsed centuries ago by earthquake or cannon -- the histories disagree, and the ruin is more eloquent than any reconstruction. Stone nagas guard the stairways, their serpent bodies eroded into smoothness. A Buddha sits in a niche on the eastern face, serene and golden against the ancient brick, and the scale of the thing makes Lena understand something about ambition and impermanence she has been circling for years.

She photographs it from twelve angles. Callum watches her work, and she is aware of being watched, which changes the way she moves -- more deliberate, her choices more considered, as though his attention has increased the resolution of her own seeing.

"You tilt your head before you raise the camera," he says. "Like you're catching a frequency. Then you move to where it's strongest."

No one has ever described her process in the language of instinct. She has her own technical vocabulary -- composition, the rule of thirds, its breaking -- but he has bypassed all of it and gone to the thing underneath, and she feels suddenly, uncomfortably seen.

"That's a strange thing to notice."

"Noticing is the only skill that transfers across every country I've lived in."

They walk the old city. This becomes the shape of their days -- no plan beyond the next temple, the next meal, the next corner. Wat Phan Tao, where the wooden chapel sits like a jewel box in a garden. Wat Chiang Man, the oldest temple in the city, its elephant-base chedi circled by stone pachyderms holding up the sky. They sit on a bench at the Three Kings Monument and eat mango sticky rice from a plastic bag.

"Travel is escapism," she says. "We're both here because we're not somewhere else."

"That's reductive. You could say that about any location."

"Most people have a place they'd call home. We have itineraries."

He is quiet. He pulls the sticky rice apart with his fingers, methodical, the same focused attention he gives everything. Then he says, without preparation:

"My father died last year. He was in Edinburgh. I was in Seoul. I made it back for the funeral but not for the dying, which my mother has not forgiven me for, and which I have not forgiven myself for, and we are now performing a very Scottish dance around this unforgiveness where no one mentions it and everyone feels it."

"I'm sorry."

"I'm telling you because you asked a real question. Most people ask travel questions -- where have you been, where are you going. You asked a home question. I don't go home because home is now a place where my father isn't and my mother's silence is, and Seoul is far enough that the silence doesn't carry."

She wants to touch him. She does not. Thirty-six hours is not enough time to earn that gesture, and she has learned the cost of reaching for people before the distance has been measured.

Instead she tells him about David. Not the whole story -- just the outline. David, who she lived with in Portland for four years. Kind, stable, certain. David wanted a house and a dog and a life that stayed in one place, and she left not because he was wrong but because she was the wrong person for the life he was right about. Five months of mutual silence since.

"So we're both running," Callum says.

"I prefer 'traveling.'"

"That's the same thing with better luggage."

She laughs. The big laugh, the one her mother warned her about. He grins, and she sees his face crack open -- the careful observer replaced, for a moment, by someone younger and less defended. She realizes he has been performing too, and that beneath the performance is a man who is tired and sad and far from home and laughing because she made him laugh.

The cooking class is her idea. Khun Noi runs it from a wooden Lanna house near the east gate, with a garden where the ingredients grow. Khun Noi is sixty and built like a compressed spring. She teaches with the authority of a surgeon, her knife moving through lemongrass and galangal as though the vegetables have already agreed to be cut.

"You pound too gentle," Khun Noi tells Callum at the mortar and pestle. "Thai cooking is not meditation. You must be angry at the chili."

"I'm not angry at anything."

"Then think of something. Then pound."

He pounds. The paste improves. Lena catches his eye across the kitchen and he mouths something she cannot hear, and she does not need to hear it to know it is funny, and this -- this private language built from nothing in less than two days -- is the thing that frightens her. Not because it is happening but because of how easily it is happening. In her experience, the effortless things are the ones that cannot last.

They eat what they have cooked on the floor of Khun Noi's kitchen. Tom kha gai, green curry, the papaya salad Lena made, which Khun Noi concedes is "not terrible." The food is good because they made it and because eating on the floor of a stranger's kitchen in a city where neither of them lives seasons everything with the flavor of the unrepeatable.

After, they walk along the moat as the sun sets. The water is still and green, reflecting the old city wall, and the light is the kind photographers live for -- golden, low, turning everything it touches into a better version of itself.

"I need to ask you something," he says.

"Okay."

"Are we doing something here? Or are we two people who happen to be in the same city?"

She stops walking. The moat stretches ahead, the eastern gate visible in the distance, ancient brick framing modern traffic.

"Does it matter?"

"Yes. Because I'm leaving in seven days, and you're leaving in ten, and if we're doing something, then we're doing it with a deadline, and I'd like to know."

"You'd like to know the terms."

"I'm being direct because indirect takes longer and we've established that time is limited."

She looks at the moat. A long-tailed boat is tied to the bank, rocking in some imperceptible current. She thinks about David and his house and his life that stayed. She thinks about her mother's voice. She thinks about the light and the thing it touches.

"We're doing something," she says.

He does not kiss her. He takes her hand -- without ceremony, the way you pick up something that has been waiting for you -- and they walk along the moat in the last of the light, and the city hums around them, and neither says what they are both thinking, which is that seven days is not enough and ten days is not enough, and that this is the central problem of wanting someone in a place you are about to leave.
