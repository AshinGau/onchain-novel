Bootstrap Chapter 3: The Countdown to Departure

Five days left and they have stopped counting separately. His nine and her twelve have merged into a single number -- five, the days until he flies back to Seoul -- and this shared arithmetic is itself a kind of intimacy. They have keys to nothing. They have five days.

They rent a motorbike and ride north into the hills. Callum drives. Lena sits behind him with her arms around his waist and her camera bag pressed between them, and the road curves through rice paddies and teak forests and small villages where dogs sleep in the middle of the asphalt. The air at altitude is cooler, thinner, carrying pine and wood smoke and something she can only describe as green -- the exhalation of a mountain that has been breathing longer than any country that has claimed it.

They stop at a village where a Karen woman sells coffee she has grown and roasted herself. The coffee tastes like the earth it came from -- mineral, deep, with a sweetness that arrives late. They sit on a bamboo platform overlooking a valley and drink it, and the woman asks in Thai if they are married.

Callum says "mai chai." Not "mai." Not "plao." He chose the negation that means not quite, not exactly -- the form that leaves a door open. She wonders if he knows he did this or if the language made the choice for him, the way languages sometimes know what their speakers do not.

"What did she ask?" Lena says, though she understood.

"If we're together."

"And what did you say?"

"I said no."

She lets it pass. But she keeps the "mai chai" -- keeps it the way you keep a coin from a country you intend to return to.

Four days. They visit the elephant sanctuary, the rescue kind where you walk beside them and watch them move through forest with the slow majesty of creatures who remember being wild. An elephant takes a banana from Lena's hand with a trunk that is simultaneously the most delicate and most powerful thing she has ever touched, and she cries, and Callum does not ask why. His not-asking is a form of understanding she has not experienced before -- David would have asked, would have needed to know, would have tried to fix the crying, and Callum simply stands beside her while the tears run their course. This is either emotional intelligence or emotional distance, and she cannot yet tell which, and the not-knowing is part of the attraction.

Three days. Rain. The monsoon is weeks away but the sky sends an advance party, a downpour that turns the moat to chaos and empties the streets. They stay in her room at the guesthouse. They read -- photography journal, Murakami -- and they do not talk for two hours, and the silence holds no obligation. It is not comfortable silence, which implies effort. It is simply silence, shared.

That evening, on the guesthouse roof, he tells her about the notebook. He has been carrying it for six years -- every country, every city. Not a journal. A collection of first sentences for novels he will never write. He shows her. The handwriting is small and precise, and the sentences are good -- sharp, surprising, full of the same noticing he does in conversation. One reads: "The translator was fired for accuracy." Another: "She kept his letters in the order she wished she'd received them."

"These are better than first sentences," she says. "These are whole stories."

"That's the problem. The first sentence is the only part I'm good at. Everything after is just -- continuation. And continuation terrifies me."

He taps his thumbnail against his teeth. She recognizes the gesture now -- the sound of Callum approaching honesty.

"That's not about writing," she says.

"No," he says. "It's not."

Two days. The Saturday walking street at Wualai -- silver jewelry, hand-woven cotton, wood carvings, the visual vocabulary of northern Thailand compressed into a mile of lantern-lit commerce. Callum buys a small silver ring from a hill tribe woman and puts it on his left pinkie without comment. Lena sees it and says nothing. The ring becomes another word in the language they are building, composed entirely of things unsaid.

That night, in the dark of her room, she asks: "What happens after Seoul?"

"I teach. I read books. I write first sentences. I think about a woman I met on a songthaew."

"Be serious."

"I am serious. That's the whole of my foreseeable future. I have no five-year plan. I have a lease that renews monthly and a job that could replace me in a week. My life is designed for leaving."

"So is mine."

"I know. That's the problem, isn't it? Two lives designed for leaving, and neither of us can figure out how to stay."

Day one. His last day.

They ride to Doi Inthanon, the highest peak in Thailand, two hours south. The road climbs through cloud forest into air cold enough that Lena wishes she had brought a jacket. Callum gives her his -- a green canvas thing that smells like him, soap and coffee and a warmth she has come to know through five days of proximity that is not enough time and is exactly enough time.

At the summit, the clouds are below them. She has never stood above the sky before. The twin chedis built for the king and queen sit on adjacent peaks, surrounded by gardens that bloom in defiance of altitude, and she wants to photograph everything and cannot photograph any of it. The camera would make it smaller. Some things refuse the frame.

On the ride back, they do not discuss tomorrow. They talk about books and food and a temple they never visited and the way light falls through teak shutters at four in the afternoon. They talk about everything except flight number TG2145, departing eleven-forty, Gate 4, a series of numbers that have become the coordinates of an ending.

That evening they walk to Wat Lok Molee, on the northern edge of the old city. The chedi is illuminated, gold on dark. The grounds are empty, the monks retired, the tourists drawn to louder lights. They sit on the steps of the viharn.

"I didn't leave David because I didn't love him," she says. "I left because I loved him exactly the right amount for the life he wanted, and I couldn't breathe inside it. He wanted a house. I wanted a frame -- a rectangle to point at things and say, this, look at this. Those aren't compatible geometries."

"And now?"

"Now I have met someone who makes me want both, and he is leaving tomorrow, and the timing is either terrible or perfect."

"Come to Seoul," he says.

"Don't."

"I'm serious."

"That's why I'm saying don't. Because if you say it and I say yes, I'm a woman who followed a man to a country. If you say it and I say no, this ends with a refusal. And I want it to end with something that isn't a door closing."

"What does a door not-closing look like?"

She thinks. The temple is quiet. The city breathes around them -- jasmine and exhaust and cooking smoke and a distant chant she cannot place.

"It looks like this. Two people on temple steps, not making promises they can't keep. Knowing this happened and that it mattered and that mattering is not the same as lasting."

"That sounds like giving up."

"Maybe. Or maybe it sounds like the first sentence of something, and neither of us knows the rest yet."

He looks at her. She has used his language -- the notebook, the first sentences, the terror of continuation -- and she watches him hear it, watches the recognition move across his face.

They sit until the temple lights go off. Then they walk back through the old city, past the moat where they first held hands, past the corner where their streets diverge, and this time they do not diverge.

In the morning she does not go to the airport. She decides at four a.m., in the gray light, listening to him breathe. She will not watch him walk through security. She will not stand at the glass. These are rituals for ordinary departures, and this is not an ordinary departure.

He packs his bag. The paperback goes in last, face down, like a secret. He stands at her door and they look at each other across five feet of teak floor that is also five days and also the distance between Edinburgh and Portland and also no distance at all.

"The light or the thing the light touches," he says.

"I still don't know."

"Neither do I. But I think the question is the point."

He leaves. She hears his footsteps on the stairs, the creak of the front door, a tuk-tuk starting. Then nothing. The city begins its morning -- monks chanting, roosters, the clatter of a breakfast cart, the first motorbikes coughing to life.

Lena picks up her camera. She walks out into Chiang Mai, which is still here, which does not leave. She walks along the moat in the early light and photographs it -- the water, the sky, the space between the question and the answer where everything that matters lives.

On her phone, a message. Timestamped six minutes ago.

"Not good at endings so I'm calling this an interruption. When you figure out the light thing let me know. Someone in Seoul will be reading Murakami and thinking about coffee stains and laughs that are the right size."

She reads it twice. She does not reply. Not yet.

She walks east, toward the sun, toward the temples, toward whatever comes next.
