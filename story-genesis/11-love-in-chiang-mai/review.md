### Final Assessment
Love in Chiang Mai delivers a strong genesis for collaborative continuation. The central premise -- two travelers whose identities are built on impermanence, confronting an attraction that demands something permanent -- gives the romance both its engine and its obstacle. The story's signature devices work in concert: the "light or the thing the light touches" question threads through all three chapters as both photography metaphor and relationship problem; the notebook of first sentences makes Callum's fear of continuation concrete and thematic; the "mai chai" language observation turns a single Thai word into a window on self-knowledge. Lena is a photographer who refuses to photograph faces, who left a man she loved because she couldn't breathe inside his certainty, who grabs her camera back from a stranger's steadying hand -- a character built on the tension between seeing clearly and being seen. Callum is articulate about everything except what matters, a man who collects beginnings and flees continuations, whose grief over his father is inseparable from his geography. Chiang Mai is rendered with sensory specificity -- the alms round, the cooking class, the moat at sunset, the mountain above the clouds -- and functions as the third character, the place that stays when people leave. The ending is open without being evasive: the text message, the unanswered question, the eastward walk. Ready for publication.
