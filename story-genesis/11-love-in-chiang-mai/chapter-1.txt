Chapter 1: Meeting on the Road

The songthaew lurches and her coffee lifts from the cup. Not a splash -- a clean arc of dark liquid that hangs in the air for one impossible second before landing on the knee of the man sitting across from her. He looks down at the stain spreading across his linen trousers as though a small country has just appeared on a map he was reading.

"That's hot," he says, in English. Not a complaint. An observation.

"I'm so sorry." Lena is already pulling napkins from her bag, the rough brown kind she took from the 7-Eleven on Tha Phae Road. She holds them out. He takes them, and their fingers touch over the wet paper, and she notices his hands -- long-fingered, ink-stained at the right index, a writer's callus or an artist's. He dabs at the stain with the calm of someone who has ruined better clothes.

"It's fine. These were already losing the argument with Chiang Mai." He gestures at the trousers, and she sees he is right -- the hem is frayed, the fabric soft from too many hand washes in hotel sinks. Clothes that have been traveling longer than their owner intended.

The songthaew grinds up the hill toward Doi Suthep. Around them, the city falls away in terracotta roofs and temple spires, the old city moat catching the late-afternoon light like a ribbon of copper. Lena has been in Chiang Mai for six days. She tells herself she is here to photograph the Lanna architecture for her portfolio. This is true in the way that most reasons for travel are true -- it is the reason she bought the ticket, not the reason she stays.

"First time?" he asks.

"Here? Yes. Thailand, no. I lived in Bangkok for a semester in university." She pauses. "You?"

"Third time in Chiang Mai. I keep leaving and the city keeps being here when I come back, so I think the attachment is one-sided."

She laughs. It comes out louder than she intends, and two Thai women in the back of the songthaew glance over. Lena covers her mouth, an old habit -- her mother always said her laugh was too big for rooms. But they are not in a room. They are on a red truck climbing a mountain road with the wind pulling at their hair and the jungle pressing in from both sides, and her laugh is exactly the right size for this.

His name is Callum. Scottish, though the accent has been sanded down by years of living elsewhere -- she catches it only in certain vowels, the way "here" becomes something closer to a sigh. He teaches English at a language school in Seoul. Has done for three years. Before that, Tokyo. Before that, a master's degree in literature he has never used for its intended purpose, which he describes as "an expensive way to learn I preferred reading to writing about reading." He has a habit of tapping his thumbnail against his teeth when he is thinking, a small percussive tic that she will come to recognize as the sound of Callum deciding whether to say something honest.

The songthaew stops at the base of the naga staircase. Three hundred and nine steps, the guidebook says. Lena has read the guidebook. Callum, she suspects, has not.

They do not plan to climb together. It simply happens -- he lingers at the ticket booth searching for change, she waits because it would feel strange to walk away mid-sentence, and then they are climbing side by side as though someone had decided the photographer from Portland and the teacher from Edinburgh should share these particular stairs on this particular afternoon.

The steps are steep. Lena counts without meaning to. Callum talks without seeming to need breath, and she learns things in the scattered, out-of-order way you learn a person when you have no context: he reads mostly Japanese fiction in translation, he cannot eat coriander because of a gene he is very specific about, he once got lost in Kyoto for an entire day and describes it as one of the best things that has happened to him.

"Getting lost is only good when you're not trying to be found," she says, and he looks at her with a sharpness she did not expect -- as though she has said something that means more than she intended.

At the top, Wat Phra That Doi Suthep opens before them in gold and glass and late-afternoon shadow. The chedi blazes in the lowering sun, and the air smells of incense and frangipani and the particular green exhalation of the mountain -- wet leaves, moss, the breath of trees that have been here longer than the temple. Monks in saffron move through the courtyard. Tourists circle the chedi clockwise, some praying, some photographing, some doing both at once.

Lena lifts her camera. She does not photograph Callum. She photographs the space around him -- the gold tiles reflecting light onto the side of his face, the line of prayer bells behind his shoulder, the way his shadow falls across the marble floor and merges with the shadow of a pillar. Later, when she scrolls through the images, he will be in every frame without being the subject of any.

"What are you shooting?" he asks.

"The light."

"The light or the thing the light touches?"

She lowers the camera. "That's the same question."

"No," he says. "It isn't."

A tourist bumps Lena's elbow, jostling her camera. Callum's hand shoots out -- steadies her wrist, firm and quick. She pulls her arm back harder than necessary.

"I had it."

"I know. Reflex."

"It's a camera, not a child."

He raises both hands in mock surrender, and she sees that she has been sharp, sharper than the moment warranted, and she understands why: his hand on her wrist felt proprietary, and she does not belong to anyone, and the speed of her reaction tells her something about herself she is not ready to examine.

They stand at the terrace wall and look out over Chiang Mai. The city is a grid of lights beginning to appear in the dusk, the moat a dark square at its center, the Ping River threading south. The mountains are turning purple. Somewhere below, the night bazaar is opening, vendors unfolding tables and hanging lanterns, the smell of grilled pork and mango sticky rice beginning its nightly campaign.

"How long are you here?" he asks.

"Twelve more days. I have a flight from Bangkok on the fourteenth."

"Twelve days." He says it the way you say a number that is simultaneously too large and too small. "I have nine."

She does not ask what comes after nine. He does not ask what waits in Portland. These are the questions that close conversations, and neither of them wants this one closed.

The songthaew back down is crowded. They stand at the back, shoulders touching when the truck takes the curves, and the city rises to meet them -- outer ring road, moat, the old city where temples sit among guesthouses and coffee shops like grandparents at a children's party.

He is staying on Moon Muang Road. She is on Ratchadamnoen, two blocks away -- the natural geometry of budget travelers gravitating to the same quarter-mile radius.

"I was going to eat at the night bazaar," she says.

"I was going to eat anywhere."

"Those are compatible plans."

The night bazaar is a river of light and commerce. They wade in. Lena buys a paper lantern she does not need. Callum buys a wooden elephant he admits is for no one. They eat khao soi from a stall where the woman who serves them has been making the same dish in the same spot for, she tells them, twenty-two years. The curry is rich and coconut-heavy, the noodles both crispy and soft, and Callum eats with the focused attention of someone who believes food is a serious subject.

"This is better than anything in Seoul," he says.

"That's disloyal."

"Loyalty to cuisine is negotiable. That's the whole point of travel."

They walk through the old city after. The moat is lit by streetlamps, the water dark and still, reflecting the lights as smeared gold. A temple cat watches them from a wall. The air has cooled into something softer, carrying jasmine and exhaust and the distant thump of music from a rooftop bar.

Lena tells him about the project. Freelance architectural photography -- she has a commission from a design magazine to shoot Lanna temple architecture, the wooden gables and layered roofs, the way northern Thai builders married teak to tile. Real work, real pay. But also the kind of work that can be done from anywhere, which means it is the kind of work that keeps her from choosing a where.

"Portland is home?" he asks.

"Portland is where my apartment is. I'm not sure those are the same thing."

He taps his thumbnail against his teeth. "The Japanese have a word -- furusato. The place you're from, but with longing attached. Except some of us have the longing without the place."

She looks at him. "That's a very practiced thing to say."

"It is," he admits. "I've said it before. Does that make it less true?"

"It makes me wonder who you said it to."

He holds her gaze. "No one who heard it."

They reach the corner where their streets diverge. Ratchadamnoen goes left; Moon Muang goes straight.

"There's a temple I want to see tomorrow," he says. "Wat Chedi Luang. The ruined one. They used to keep the Emerald Buddha there before Bangkok took it."

"Bangkok didn't take it. Laos had it, then Bangkok had it. It's had more addresses than I have."

He smiles. "Come with me. Morning. Before the heat."

It is not a date. It is not not a date. It is the thing that happens between travelers -- an agreement to share time in a place where time is the only currency either of them is spending.

"Eight o'clock," she says.

"Seven."

"Seven-thirty."

"Done."

He turns and walks up Moon Muang Road. She watches for a moment -- the tall frame, the easy stride, the paperback visible in his back pocket. Then she turns left toward her guesthouse, where the owner's dog sleeps across the doorway and the room smells of teak and mosquito coil, and she lies on the bed without showering and looks at the ceiling fan turning and thinks about the difference between the light and the thing the light touches, and how he was right -- they are not the same question at all.
