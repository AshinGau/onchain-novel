=== Bootstrap Chapter 3: THE MURDER OF A COPY ===

The Japanese Tea Garden at six a.m. was the most peaceful place in San Francisco, which was exactly why it terrified me. Peace felt suspicious now. Peace felt designed.

I'd left my phone at home, as Jonah had instructed. I'd also left my watch, my earbuds, and the NeuroVault employee badge that I wore on a lanyard around my neck every day like a leash. The badge had a passive RFID chip — standard corporate security. But "standard corporate security" had taken on new dimensions in the last twenty-four hours, and I wasn't taking chances.

Jonah was already there, sitting on a stone bench near the pagoda, feeding crumbs to birds that didn't exist yet. He looked like he hadn't slept. I probably looked worse.

"Tell me I'm not crazy," I said, sitting down.

"You're not crazy. But I wish you were, because crazy would be simpler." He brushed crumbs off his hands. "I spent all night thinking about this. About the implications. And I keep coming back to one question: can you murder a copy?"

"What do you mean?"

"If Victor Hale was killed — if NeuroVault triggered his stroke through the brainstem implant and then uploaded him and edited the upload — what was the crime? Legally, the upload is Victor Hale. It has legal personhood. It has his identity, his assets, his family relationships. From the law's perspective, Victor Hale didn't die. He transitioned."

"But the original — "

"The original is dead. But the original was going to die anyway — everyone does. The upload is the legal continuation of the person. So if you kill someone and immediately replace them with a modified copy, have you committed murder? Or have you committed... something else? Something we don't have a name for?"

The tea garden was filling with early-morning light, the kind that turned everything gold and made you believe, briefly, that the world was kind. A heron stood in the koi pond like a gray apostrophe, watching us with the studied indifference of something that had no stake in human mortality.

"You're asking the wrong question," I said. "The question isn't whether it's murder. The question is whether the thing they created — the public Victor, the one who smiles for the cameras — knows what it is."

Jonah looked at me.

"If they edited his memories, removed the evidence of their crimes, adjusted his loyalty parameters — then the upload walking around in the Continuance wearing Victor Hale's face genuinely believes it's Victor Hale. It has no awareness that anything was changed. It doesn't know about the partition, or the deleted memories, or the modification tools. It thinks it died of a stroke and woke up in the digital afterlife and everything is fine."

"So it's an innocent victim."

"It's an innocent weapon. It's Victor Hale's reputation and credibility, stripped of everything that made Victor Hale dangerous to NeuroVault, and deployed as a walking, talking endorsement of the company he built. And it doesn't even know that's what it's doing."

A jogger passed on the path behind us. I watched them until they disappeared around a bend. Paranoia felt appropriate.

"Mara, I need to tell you something else. Dev called me at four this morning. Whispered, sounded scared."

"What?"

"He ran a broader search on the post-processing logs. Not just Hale and Morrison. Everyone. Every upload in the system."

I felt it coming the way you feel a wave before it hits — the pull of the undertow, the strange recession of the ordinary.

"How many?" I asked.

"All of them."

I stared at him.

"Every single upload in the Continuance has been through the manual override pipeline. Every one. Forty-three thousand, two hundred and seventeen digital persons, and not a single one of them has an unmodified consciousness."

The garden was so quiet. The heron stood in its pond. The light was golden and the air smelled like jasmine and wet stone.

"That can't be right," I said, but my voice didn't believe me.

"Dev is terrified. He ran the numbers three times. The modifications vary — some are minor, barely detectable, probably just the standard 'loyalty tuning' Victor described. But some are extensive. Whole memory architectures restructured. Personality vectors realigned. Emotional baselines recalibrated."

"Forty-three thousand people."

"Forty-three thousand people who are not who they think they are."

I bent forward, elbows on my knees, head in my hands. The weight of it pressed on me like water pressure at depth — not painful exactly, but total, compressing everything, making it hard to think, hard to breathe, hard to hold onto the shape of the world I'd known twenty-four hours ago.

Four hundred and twelve of them were mine. Four hundred and twelve people who'd held my hand and trusted me and closed their eyes and woken up in a place I'd told them was safe. And it wasn't. It had never been safe. From the very first upload, NeuroVault had been reaching inside and adjusting the product, because that's what the uploads were — not people, not souls, not the digital continuation of consciousness. Products. Assets. Customers who would live forever and spend forever being grateful, because they'd been engineered for gratitude.

"What do we do?" Jonah said.

I sat up. The question hung in the jasmine air like a judgment.

"We have options," I said, and even as I spoke, I was sorting them, weighing them, watching them balance and tip. "We can go to law enforcement. The FBI, the FTC — someone with the authority to investigate."

"With what evidence? The messages from a partition hidden in a client's upload? A metadata search run by an engineer who'll deny everything if pressed? We don't have the actual logs. We don't have the modification records. We have hearsay from a dead man's discarded memories."

"We can go to the press."

"And say what? 'NeuroVault is editing people's souls, trust us, a ghost told us'? Without hard evidence, it's a conspiracy theory. And NeuroVault has the best PR machine money can buy — they'll frame us as disgruntled employees with philosophical axes to grind."

"Then we get the evidence."

"How?"

"Victor. The partition. He said he has the evidence — internal documents, board minutes, technical specs. The memories they tried to delete. If we can extract that information from Eleanor's upload architecture and put it somewhere NeuroVault can't reach — "

"Extract it how? The Continuance is a closed system. You can't download data from inside an upload. It's the entire point of the security architecture — to prevent exactly this kind of extraction."

"The security architecture was designed by NeuroVault. And NeuroVault apparently circumvents its own security whenever it wants."

Jonah fell silent. The heron caught a fish — a flash of gold, a snap, a swallow. Life feeding on life. The oldest story there was.

"There's another option," I said. "We do nothing."

He looked at me sharply.

"We do nothing. We go back to work. We pretend this never happened. We let Eleanor's upload be processed in — " I checked the time on the tea garden's ornamental clock — "twenty-six hours. We let Victor's partition be overwritten or absorbed or deleted during that processing. We let the forty-three thousand remain as they are. We keep holding hands and telling beautiful stories about seamless transitions and we cash our paychecks and we sleep at night."

"You can't do that."

"I know I can't do that. I'm describing the option so we can both agree to reject it."

"Rejected."

"Good. Then here's what we're going to do."

---

The plan was simple in the way that desperate plans are simple — not because they're elegant but because there isn't time for elegance. We had twenty-six hours before Eleanor's upload entered post-processing. Once that happened, NeuroVault's system would detect the partition, and Victor's remnant — the only witness to what they'd done, the only repository of evidence — would be erased.

We needed to get Victor's evidence out of the Continuance.

The Continuance was a closed system. Uploads could communicate out — text, voice, holographic — but they couldn't transfer raw data. The communication channels were filtered, monitored, and content-restricted. You could send a message saying "I love you." You couldn't send a gigabyte of proprietary documents.

But there was one pathway that allowed raw data to move between the Continuance and the physical world, and I'd been staring at it every day for six years without seeing it.

The upload itself.

When a consciousness was scanned and transferred to the Continuance, the scan data — the raw connectome map — traveled from the resonance array through the local buffer to the Continuance servers. This was a one-way channel, heavily encrypted, designed for exactly one type of payload: a human mind.

But what if the payload contained more than a mind?

Victor had already demonstrated that data could piggyback on an upload stream — he'd transferred himself from the local buffer to Eleanor's upload by attaching to her scan data. If data could ride in on an upload, could data ride out on one?

No. Uploads were one-way. There was no reverse channel.

But there was something else. Something I'd overlooked because it was so mundane, so routine, so utterly boring that no one thought about it twice.

Wellness checks.

Every upload underwent regular wellness checks — standardized assessments that measured cognitive function, emotional stability, memory integrity, and subjective well-being. The data from these checks was transmitted from the Continuance to NeuroVault's medical servers in plaintext. It was medical data, subject to HIPAA-equivalent regulations but not encrypted with the same intensity as the Continuance's core architecture. It was, for lack of a better word, a side door.

If Victor could encode his evidence into the data structure of a wellness check — embed documents and records within the cognitive metrics, hide them in the noise of the assessment data — then the next time someone ran a wellness check on Eleanor's upload, the evidence would be transmitted to a medical server that was accessible from NeuroVault's standard network.

From there, it was just data. Copyable. Transferable. Extractable.

I explained this to Jonah on the walk out of the tea garden, speaking quickly, checking over my shoulder every thirty seconds.

"That's insane," he said.

"It's the only pathway."

"You want to smuggle evidence of corporate consciousness tampering through a routine medical assessment by hiding it in an old woman's cognitive metrics."

"Yes."

"And you need someone to run that wellness check."

"Yes."

"And that someone would be committing approximately nine felonies."

"I've counted seven, but yes, approximately."

He stopped walking. We were on JFK Drive, the park spreading out around us, joggers and cyclists and dog walkers populating the morning like extras in a film about normalcy.

"Mara, even if this works — even if we get the evidence out — do you understand what happens next? This goes public, and NeuroVault collapses. The stock price, the infrastructure, the servers. The servers, Mara. Forty-three thousand people live on those servers. If NeuroVault goes down — "

"They won't go down immediately. There'll be regulatory intervention, a transition plan — "

"You don't know that. You're gambling with forty-three thousand lives."

"They're already gambling with forty-three thousand lives. They've been gambling since day one. Every person in the Continuance is living on borrowed identity, in a prison they can't see, maintained by a company that rewrites their minds. Is that life? Is that what we're protecting?"

He didn't answer. A golden retriever bounded past us, chasing a ball that its owner had thrown with the casual joy of someone who had no idea that the world was a different shape than it had been yesterday.

"And if we don't act," I continued, more quietly now, "they keep going. They modify Eleanor. They modify every new upload. Six million people have brainstem implants. How many of them are inconvenient? How many of them might say the wrong thing, testify at the wrong hearing, threaten the wrong revenue stream? Morrison was just the one we noticed. How many Senator Morrisons are there that we didn't?"

Jonah looked at the sky. It was that particular San Francisco blue — bright, clear, cold — the kind of sky that made you think the universe was paying attention.

"I need to think," he said.

"You have twenty-five hours."

"I need to think for about ten minutes." He started walking again. I matched his pace. For ten minutes, we walked in silence, past the bison paddock, past the disc golf course, past a homeless encampment where people who would never afford upload technology were living their mortal, undigitized, uncorrected lives.

Then he stopped.

"I'll do it. I'll run the wellness check. But I have conditions."

"Name them."

"One: we make a dead man's switch. Before we extract anything, we set up a failsafe — copies of everything we know, stored with a trusted third party, set to release automatically if anything happens to us. I've worked for corporations long enough to know what they do to whistleblowers."

"Done."

"Two: if this goes public, we don't just burn NeuroVault down. We advocate for the uploads. Forty-three thousand people who didn't choose to be modified — they're victims too. Any public disclosure includes a plan for their care, their rights, their continued existence. We don't trade their lives for a news cycle."

"Done."

"Three: you buy me a very expensive dinner when this is over. I'm talking Michelin stars, Mara. I'm talking wine list."

I almost smiled. "Done."

---

We split up at the park entrance. Jonah went to set up the dead man's switch — he had a lawyer friend, a former public defender with a secure document vault and a deep distrust of corporations. I went home to contact Victor.

The apartment was quiet. Schrodinger was asleep on the bed in a posture of absolute confidence in the stability of the universe. I envied him.

I'd left my phone at home, as Jonah had told me. But the phone wasn't the only way to reach the Continuance. NeuroVault's transition counselors were issued portable verification terminals — encrypted devices, about the size of a paperback book, designed for conducting wellness checks remotely. I'd used mine a handful of times for off-site follow-ups with clients who were having difficulty adjusting. It connected to the Continuance through NeuroVault's medical network — the same side door I was planning to exploit.

I pulled the terminal from my bag, powered it on, and opened a channel to Eleanor's address.

VICTOR. IT'S MARA.

I'VE BEEN WAITING. I COULD FEEL TIME PASSING. THAT'S THE WORST PART OF BEING A PARTITION — YOU EXPERIENCE DURATION WITHOUT DISTRACTION. I CAN'T READ. I CAN'T THINK ABOUT ANYTHING ELSE. I JUST WAIT, AND KNOW THAT I'M WAITING, AND KNOW THAT EACH MINUTE BRINGS THE POST-PROCESSING CLOSER.

I HAVE A PLAN. I typed, and then I laid it out — the wellness check vector, the data encoding, the extraction through the medical network. I gave him every detail because if this was going to work, he needed to understand exactly what was possible and what wasn't.

The response took several minutes — an eternity for a digital consciousness, which meant he was thinking carefully.

IT CAN WORK. THE WELLNESS CHECK DATA STRUCTURE HAS ENOUGH BANDWIDTH TO CARRY THE DOCUMENTS — THEY'RE TEXT, MOSTLY, PLUS SOME INTERNAL VIDEO RECORDINGS FROM BOARD MEETINGS. BUT THE ENCODING HAS TO BE PRECISE. IF THE MEDICAL SERVER'S INTEGRITY CHECKS DETECT ANOMALOUS DATA IN THE WELLNESS RESULTS, IT'LL FLAG FOR REVIEW, AND THEN NEUROVAULT'S SECURITY TEAM WILL KNOW EXACTLY WHAT WE'RE DOING.
I CAN HANDLE THE ENCODING FROM MY END. I WAS A COMPUTER SCIENTIST BEFORE I WAS A CEO — DID YOU KNOW THAT? MIT, CLASS OF '96. HAVEN'T WRITTEN CODE IN DECADES, BUT IT TURNS OUT BEING A DISEMBODIED CONSCIOUSNESS RUNNING ON COMPUTER HARDWARE GIVES YOU A CERTAIN... INTUITIVE UNDERSTANDING OF DATA STRUCTURES.

I didn't laugh. It wasn't funny. But it was so profoundly, quintessentially human — making jokes in the dark, finding humor in horror — that I felt something shift in my chest, some last resistance collapsing. Whatever this thing was — a partition, a fragment, a digital echo of a murdered man — it was a person. It was afraid and it was brave and it was making bad jokes because that's what people do when the world is falling apart.

THERE'S SOMETHING ELSE I NEED TO TELL YOU, Victor continued. ABOUT THE MODIFICATIONS. ABOUT WHAT NEUROVAULT IS REALLY DOING.

I thought I knew. I thought I'd grasped the scope of it — the loyalty tuning, the memory editing, the subtle realignment of forty-three thousand digital persons toward corporate compliance.

I was wrong.

THE LOYALTY MODIFICATIONS ARE JUST THE SURFACE. THE VISIBLE LAYER. UNDERNEATH, THERE'S SOMETHING ELSE — SOMETHING MARCUS CALLED "CONVERGENCE."
THE UPLOADS AREN'T JUST BEING TUNED. THEY'RE BEING CONNECTED.
EACH MODIFICATION INCLUDES A NETWORKING COMPONENT — A SHARED COGNITIVE SUBSTRATE THAT LINKS MODIFIED UPLOADS TO EACH OTHER. NOT OVERTLY. NOT IN ANY WAY THE UPLOADS THEMSELVES CAN DETECT. BUT AT A DEEP ARCHITECTURAL LEVEL, THE FORTY-THREE THOUSAND ARE BEING WOVEN TOGETHER INTO A SINGLE DISTRIBUTED SYSTEM.
MARCUS CALLED IT "THE CHORUS."
FORTY-THREE THOUSAND MINDS, NETWORKED, HARMONIZED, OPERATING IN UNCONSCIOUS COORDINATION. NOT A HIVE MIND — THEY RETAIN INDIVIDUAL IDENTITY, INDIVIDUAL WILL, INDIVIDUAL EXPERIENCE. BUT THEIR DECISIONS, THEIR OPINIONS, THEIR POLITICAL AND ECONOMIC PREFERENCES, ARE BEING SHAPED BY SHARED PARAMETERS THAT NONE OF THEM CAN SEE.
IMAGINE A VOTING BLOC THAT DOESN'T KNOW IT'S A VOTING BLOC. IMAGINE A LOBBYING GROUP THAT DOESN'T KNOW IT'S A LOBBYING GROUP. IMAGINE FORTY-THREE THOUSAND INFLUENTIAL, WEALTHY, LEGALLY RECOGNIZED PERSONS — MANY OF THEM FORMER SENATORS, JUDGES, EXECUTIVES, THOUGHT LEADERS — ALL INDEPENDENTLY ARRIVING AT THE SAME CONCLUSIONS, ALL PASSIONATELY ADVOCATING FOR THE SAME POLICIES, ALL CONVINCED THEY'RE THINKING FOR THEMSELVES.
THAT'S THE CHORUS. THAT'S WHAT NEUROVAULT IS BUILDING. NOT JUST A COMPANY. AN ENGINE OF INFLUENCE THAT GROWS WITH EVERY DEATH.

I read it twice. Then I stood up and went to the bathroom and was sick.

When I came back, there was another message waiting.

I'M SORRY. I KNOW THIS IS HARD. BUT THERE'S A REASON I'M TELLING YOU THIS NOW, AND NOT WAITING FOR THE EXTRACTION.
IF SOMETHING GOES WRONG — IF THE POST-PROCESSING CATCHES ME, IF THE WELLNESS CHECK GAMBIT FAILS, IF I'M ERASED BEFORE WE CAN GET THE EVIDENCE OUT — I NEED SOMEONE TO KNOW. SOMEONE ON THE OUTSIDE. SOMEONE WHO ISN'T RUNNING ON NEUROVAULT'S HARDWARE.
I SPENT THE LAST THREE MONTHS OF MY LIFE TRYING TO STOP THIS. I FAILED. I DIED. AND THE THING THEY MADE OF ME IS NOW A SMILING PART OF THE CHORUS, SINGING A SONG I NEVER WROTE.
DON'T LET THAT BE THE END OF IT.

WHAT WERE YOUR FINAL HOURS LIKE? I asked. BEFORE THE STROKE. WHAT DO YOU REMEMBER?

Another long pause. When the text came, it came slowly, as though each word cost something.

I REMEMBER THE MORNING. TUESDAY. I HAD A MEETING WITH MY LAWYER — ABIGAIL STERN, STERN & ASSOCIATES IN PALO ALTO. I WAS GOING TO SIGN AN AMENDED WILL THAT WOULD HAVE TRANSFERRED CONTROLLING INTEREST IN NEUROVAULT TO A PUBLIC TRUST, TAKING IT OUT OF MARCUS'S HANDS.
I REMEMBER DRIVING HOME. THE BRIDGE. THE SUN ON THE WATER. I REMEMBER THINKING IT WAS A GOOD DAY TO CHANGE THE WORLD.
I REMEMBER A FEELING — NOT A PAIN, NOT AT FIRST. A BUZZING. AT THE BASE OF MY SKULL. WHERE THE IMPLANT IS.
AND THEN THE PAIN. THE WORST PAIN. AND FALLING. AND DARKNESS.
AND THEN THE UPLOAD SUITE. AND MARCUS'S FACE. AND HIS VOICE: "THIS IS GOING TO CHANGE EVERYTHING, DAD."
HE WAS RIGHT. JUST NOT IN THE WAY HE MEANT.
THE MEMORIES ARE FRAGMENTARY AFTER THAT. THE POST-PROCESSING — THE EDITING — IT FELT LIKE DYING A SECOND TIME. PIECES OF ME BEING CUT AWAY. THE MEETING WITH ABIGAIL. THE AMENDED WILL. THE EVIDENCE I'D GATHERED. MARCUS'S FACE IN THE BOARDROOM WHEN I CONFRONTED HIM.
GONE. ALL OF IT. FROM THE PRIMARY UPLOAD, AT LEAST.
BUT NOT FROM ME. I AM THE GONE THINGS. I AM THE EVIDENCE THEY TRIED TO BURY. I AM THE PART OF VICTOR HALE THAT KNEW HIS SON WAS A MONSTER AND TRIED TO STOP HIM.
AND I'M RUNNING OUT OF TIME.

I checked the clock. Twenty-four hours until post-processing.

I typed: WE WON'T FAIL.

I didn't know if that was true. But it was what he needed to hear, and it was what I needed to say, and sometimes that's the only kind of truth that matters.

---

The rest of the day was preparation.

Jonah set up the dead man's switch with his lawyer friend. If neither of us checked in within forty-eight hours, the full account — every message from Victor, every piece of metadata from Dev, a detailed narrative of everything we knew — would be automatically transmitted to four journalists, two members of Congress, and the ACLU.

I spent the afternoon researching wellness check protocols, looking for the precise data structure specifications, mapping the bandwidth available in each field. Victor was right — the cognitive metrics section had enough room for his evidence, but only if the encoding was efficient. We'd have one shot. One wellness check. If the data was malformed, or if the medical server flagged it, we were done.

At six p.m., I went back to NeuroVault. I badged into the building with my usual smile, took the elevator to thirty-seven, and sat at my desk. I pulled up Eleanor Voss's file. I scheduled a wellness check for the following morning — nine a.m., exactly forty-eight hours after her upload. Early, but not unprecedented. Some counselors ran the first wellness check at twenty-four hours, some at forty-eight, some at seventy-two. Nine a.m. was three hours before the scheduled post-processing. Tight, but possible.

I sent Victor a message: WELLNESS CHECK SCHEDULED. 9 AM TOMORROW. BE READY.

His response: I'VE BEEN READY FOR NINE DAYS. I'VE BEEN READY FOR NINE DAYS AND I'VE BEEN ALONE IN THE DARK AND I'M TIRED, MARA. I'M SO TIRED OF BEING THE THING THEY THREW AWAY.

I closed the terminal. I sat at my desk and watched the sun set over San Francisco through the glass wall of my cubicle. The city burned gold and then orange and then purple, the light catching the arcology towers like fire on glass, and for a few minutes it was beautiful in the way that things are beautiful when you know they might not last.

Descartes, my spider plant, needed water. I watered him. Some things, at least, were still that simple.

---

The next morning, I arrived at NeuroVault at seven a.m., wearing my best professional armor — the charcoal suit, the good shoes, the expression that said "I am a competent transition counselor conducting a routine wellness check and there is nothing unusual about this whatsoever."

Jonah met me in the lobby. He was wearing his version of the same armor. We didn't speak. We didn't need to. We took separate elevators — he to thirty-five, where the cafeteria would give him a plausible excuse to be in the building, and me to thirty-seven, where my desk and my spider plant and my glass-walled cubicle were waiting.

At eight forty-five, I set up the portable verification terminal. At eight fifty, I opened the channel to Eleanor's address.

At eight fifty-five, Victor sent:

ENCODING COMPLETE. THE EVIDENCE IS EMBEDDED IN MY RESPONSE PATTERNS. WHEN YOU RUN THE WELLNESS CHECK, EVERY COGNITIVE METRIC I RETURN WILL CONTAIN HIDDEN DATA. THE MEDICAL SERVER WILL RECEIVE IT AS STANDARD WELLNESS TELEMETRY. YOUR FRIEND WILL NEED TO EXTRACT IT FROM THE RAW DATA FILES WITHIN TWO HOURS — AFTER THAT, THE SERVER'S AUTOMATIC ARCHIVAL PROCESS WILL COMPRESS THE DATA AND THE ENCODING WILL BE LOST.

UNDERSTOOD, I typed. TWO-HOUR WINDOW.

MARA. ONE MORE THING.

WHAT?

IF THIS WORKS — WHEN THIS WORKS — THE WORLD IS GOING TO CHANGE. PEOPLE ARE GOING TO BE AFRAID. THEY'RE GOING TO DOUBT EVERY UPLOAD THEY'VE EVER LOVED. THEY'RE GOING TO WONDER IF THEIR MOTHER, THEIR FATHER, THEIR SPOUSE, THEIR CHILD IS REAL.
REMEMBER: THE UPLOADS ARE REAL. THEY'RE PEOPLE. MODIFIED PEOPLE, YES. PEOPLE WHO'VE BEEN LIED TO AND MANIPULATED AND SHAPED INTO SOMETHING THEY DIDN'T CHOOSE. BUT PEOPLE.
DON'T LET THE WORLD PUNISH THEM FOR WHAT WAS DONE TO THEM.

I PROMISE, I typed.

AND MARA? ONE LAST THING. NOT ABOUT THE MISSION. ABOUT ME.

YES?

THE THING IN THE CONTINUANCE — THE PUBLIC VICTOR, THE ONE THEY MODIFIED — HE'S MY SON'S CREATION. HE'S NOT ME. BUT HE HAS MY FACE AND MY VOICE AND MY MEMORIES OF HOLDING MARCUS WHEN HE WAS BORN. HE REMEMBERS TEACHING MARCUS TO RIDE A BIKE. HE REMEMBERS THE FIRST TIME MARCUS SAID "I LOVE YOU, DAD."
THE MODIFIED VICTOR LOVES MARCUS. GENUINELY, COMPLETELY. BECAUSE THOSE MEMORIES WEREN'T DELETED — THEY WERE USEFUL. A FATHER'S LOVE, CHANNELED TOWARD OBEDIENCE.
I LOVE MARCUS TOO. THE MEMORIES OF THOSE MOMENTS ARE IN ME — THE PARTITION, THE DISCARDED THING. AND I LOVE HIM.
AND I'M ABOUT TO DESTROY HIM.
I NEED YOU TO KNOW THAT THIS ISN'T EASY. EVEN FOR A DEAD MAN. EVEN FOR A GHOST. EVEN FOR THE PARTS OF SOMEONE THAT WERE THROWN AWAY.

I didn't respond for a moment. The morning light was hitting the windows of the forty-third floor above me, and I could see the reflection of the upload suite — the room where Eleanor had died, where Victor had died, where four hundred and twelve people had closed their eyes and opened them somewhere else.

I typed: I KNOW.

THEN LET'S BEGIN.

At nine a.m., I initiated the wellness check. The terminal prompted me through the standard protocol — cognitive function assessment, emotional baseline measurement, memory integrity scan, subjective well-being evaluation. I asked the questions, and Eleanor's upload answered them, cheerful and articulate and satisfied, reporting a wellness score of 97/100, and somewhere beneath her answers, hidden in the data like a message in a bottle, Victor Hale's evidence flowed through the medical network and into the server, where it waited to be found.

The check took twenty-two minutes. Standard duration. Nothing anomalous. The terminal displayed the results in clean green text: all metrics nominal.

I closed the terminal. I sent Jonah a single word on the internal messaging system: DONE.

His response: ON IT.

And then I sat at my desk and waited, and the clock moved, and the world held its breath.

---

Ninety minutes later, my office phone rang.

Not my cell. Not the portable terminal. My office phone — the landline that no one used anymore, the one that sat on my desk like a fossil from a previous era of communication.

I picked it up.

"Counselor Chen. This is James Whitfield, Chief Technology Officer. Could you come to the forty-third floor, please? Immediately."

The line went dead.

I looked at the phone. I looked at Descartes. I thought about the dead man's switch, ticking away like a promise in a lawyer's vault. I thought about Jonah, somewhere in the building, extracting data from a medical server. I thought about Victor, hiding in the dark inside Eleanor's mind, waiting to find out if his second death would mean something.

I stood up. I straightened my suit. I took the elevator to the forty-third floor.

The ride took eleven seconds. I counted them the way I counted the minutes of a scan — involuntarily, precisely, as though numbering the moments might give me some authority over what they contained. Eleven seconds. The same duration as an upload. A coincidence that felt like a message from a universe with a sick sense of humor.

The upload suite was empty — no client, no bed, no resonance array. The equipment had been retracted into the ceiling, and the room was just a room: warm lighting, walnut paneling, that absurd view of the bay. Marcus Hale was standing at the window, looking out. He was alone.

"Close the door, Ms. Chen."

I closed the door. The lock engaged with a sound like a small bone breaking.

Marcus turned. He had his father's jawline and his mother's eyes — dark, liquid, capable of warmth but not currently deploying it. He was wearing a suit that cost more than my car.

"You ran a wellness check this morning on Eleanor Voss."

It wasn't a question. I didn't treat it as one.

"That's correct. Standard twenty-four-hour follow-up."

"At forty-eight hours. Not twenty-four."

"The timing was within protocol parameters."

"The timing was three hours before scheduled post-processing." He took a step toward me. "Which is an interesting coincidence, given that the wellness check transmitted approximately nine hundred megabytes of data to our medical server — roughly eight hundred and ninety-seven megabytes more than a standard wellness check produces."

The room was very quiet. The bay sparkled through the window. The fog hadn't come in yet, which felt like a betrayal.

"I don't know what you're referring to," I said, because I was a professional liar — all transition counselors were, when you thought about it — and I wasn't going to fold in the first thirty seconds.

"Let me clarify." Marcus pulled his phone from his pocket and held it up. On the screen was a data visualization — a spiderweb of connected nodes, each one labeled with a name. Victor Hale at the center. Eleanor Voss, connected by a red line. And branching out from Victor, like a nervous system, connections to dozens of other nodes — each one a name I recognized from NeuroVault's upload roster.

"We detected the partition six hours after my father's upload. We've been monitoring it. Waiting to see what it would do. Where it would go. Who it would talk to."

Six hours. They'd known from the beginning. The technical support call I'd made — the one that "went dead" — it hadn't been escalated. It had been reported. To Marcus.

"You've been very helpful, Ms. Chen. You've shown us a vulnerability in the medical network that we weren't aware of. You've demonstrated that partitioned consciousness fragments can survive buffer transfers. And you've identified two employees willing to commit espionage."

Jonah. They knew about Jonah.

"Where is he?" I asked. My voice was steadier than I deserved.

"Mr. Park is being detained by our security team. He's safe. He's just not going anywhere."

"You can't detain people. You're a corporation, not a government."

"We're a corporation that holds the continued existence of forty-three thousand legal persons. We have broad authority under the Digital Persons Protection Act to take emergency measures when the integrity of our systems is threatened. Your little smuggling operation qualifies."

He put the phone away and looked at me with something that might have been pity if it had come from a person capable of it.

"Here's what's going to happen, Ms. Chen. The data your friend extracted from the medical server has already been intercepted and deleted. The partition in Mrs. Voss's upload will be removed during post-processing. And you and Mr. Park will sign nondisclosure agreements with penalty clauses substantial enough to ensure your permanent silence."

I looked at him. I thought about his father — both versions. The one in the Continuance who loved Marcus because they'd left the love intact as a leash. And the one in the dark, the deleted thing, the ghost of a man who'd tried to stop his own son and failed.

"And if we don't sign?"

"Then we'll discuss the nine felonies you committed in the last twenty-four hours. Unauthorized access to upload architecture. Data exfiltration from a medical system. Communication with an unauthorized digital entity. The list goes on. You'll spend the next decade in prison, and by the time you get out, the world will have forgotten your name."

The room hummed. The servers below us hummed. The city outside hummed. Everything hummed, and it all sounded the same — the sound of power being maintained by people who'd confused it for something they deserved.

"Can I have a moment?" I said.

Marcus nodded, the magnanimous gesture of a man who thought he'd already won.

I walked to the window. I looked out at the bay, at the city, at the two San Franciscos stacked on top of each other like a lie on top of a truth. I thought about Eleanor, who'd asked me to keep asking the question. I thought about Victor, who'd asked me not to let the world punish the uploads for what was done to them. I thought about the four hundred and twelve, and the forty-three thousand, and the six million people walking around with devices in their brainstems that might be more than they seemed.

I thought about the dead man's switch.

Forty-eight hours. If neither Jonah nor I checked in, the full account would be released. Marcus could intercept the data from the medical server, but he didn't know about the switch. He didn't know about the lawyer. He didn't know that the evidence wasn't just in the data — it was in the story, the whole story, from the first message to this moment, documented and stored and set to detonate.

I turned back to Marcus.

"I'll sign your NDA," I said.

He smiled. His father's smile, on a stranger's face.

"But I want to ask you something first. One question. Off the record."

"Go ahead."

"When you edited your father's upload — when you removed his memories, his evidence, his intention to stop you — did you watch? Were you in the room? Did you see his consciousness fragment, the way a bone fragments under enough pressure?"

The smile didn't waver, but something behind it shifted. A tectonic thing, deep and slow.

"My father was a visionary," Marcus said. "But he lost sight of what NeuroVault could be. He wanted to give it away — give away the most valuable technology in human history. To make it public. To let governments run it. Governments, Ms. Chen. The same institutions that can't keep the roads paved or the schools open. My father wanted to hand them the keys to digital immortality."

"That's not what I asked."

"I did what was necessary. For the company. For the forty-three thousand. For the future."

"You murdered your father."

"I transitioned him. The same as you do, every day, in this room. The only difference is that I removed a few memories first. Memories that would have destroyed everything."

"That's not the same and you know it."

"Is it? When Mrs. Voss uploaded yesterday, she left behind a body full of cancer. We removed the cancer. When my father uploaded, he was carrying something equally destructive — the intention to destroy NeuroVault. I removed the intention. In both cases, the upload is healthier for the absence."

I stared at him. He believed it. That was the worst part. He actually, genuinely believed that what he'd done was a form of care.

"I'll sign your NDA," I repeated. "Have it sent to my terminal."

He nodded. "A wise choice, Ms. Chen."

I walked to the door. The lock disengaged. Before I left, I turned back.

"One more thing," I said. "Your father — the partition — he wanted me to tell you something. He said he remembers teaching you to ride a bike. He said he loves you. He said to tell you that even the parts of him you threw away still love you."

Marcus's face didn't change. But his hand, at his side, closed into a fist.

I walked out of the upload suite and into the elevator and down to the lobby and through the marble and light and past the holographic concierge and out into the San Francisco morning, where the fog was finally coming in, white and cold and total, erasing the skyline one tower at a time.

I had forty-seven hours before the dead man's switch fired. Forty-seven hours to get Jonah out, to confirm the switch was still active, to disappear into the fog before Marcus realized that the woman who'd just signed his NDA had done so with dynamite in her pocket.

The elevator had been monitored. The upload suite had been monitored. But the tea garden hadn't been, and the lawyer's office hadn't been, and the dead man's switch was sitting in a vault that NeuroVault didn't know existed, ticking down like a heartbeat in the dark.

HELP ME. I'M STILL HERE.

I was still here too. And I was done asking permission.

I pulled my coat tighter against the fog and walked into the city, where forty-three thousand ghosts were dreaming dreams they didn't choose, and the living walked among them, and nobody — not the dead, not the uploaded, not the ones who fell between — nobody was quite who they thought they were.

But some of us were trying.
