# Rules: The Vienna Cipher

## Setting
- **Time**: January, present day. Vienna in winter -- failing light by half past three, snow on the Ringstrasse, the city folding into darkness and discretion.
- **Primary locations**: University of Vienna (Zerndl's office), the Osterreichische Nationalbibliothek (National Library reading room), Cafe Sperl on Gumpendorfer Strasse, the Josefsplatz, the Karlskirche and its crypt, the streets of the Innere Stadt and Wieden districts. The Naschmarkt area.
- **Atmosphere**: Vienna as a city built on layers of secrecy -- Habsburg surveillance state, Metternich's informants, the tradition of saying nothing while looking beautiful. Neo-Gothic and Baroque architecture as both backdrop and metaphor. The cold is physical and institutional. Music is omnipresent -- from open windows, from memories, from an unseen violinist playing a funeral march in the dark.
- **Historical layer**: The story interweaves present-day crime with early 19th-century Vienna -- Beethoven composing the Eroica while going deaf under Metternich's police state. The Congress of Vienna, Freemasonry, political radicalism, and the use of music as a medium for hidden communication.

## Characters
- **Inspector Lena Schiller**: Bundeskriminalamt (Federal Criminal Police). Left-handed -- she weaponizes this in interrogations. Has a habit of going silent for minutes while "reading" a room (her ex-husband called it "absence," her therapist calls it dissociation). Spent three years at the BVT (Bundesamt fur Verfassungsschutz -- domestic intelligence) before transferring to criminal investigation. Those three years are undiscussed but gave her deep knowledge of codes, surveillance, and institutional secrecy. Divorced. Trained in observation by her grandmother, a war survivor. Disciplined, precise, instinctively wary of premature assumptions.
- **Sergeant Haas**: Lena's partner. Large, stolid, trusts mass over subtlety. A "man of function." His wife drags him to the Musikverein; he sleeps through the second half. Loyal and competent within his lane, but not equipped for the intellectual dimensions of this case.
- **Professor Emil Zerndl**: University of Vienna cryptographer. Vanished from his office leaving behind warm coffee, coat, wallet, keys. An overturned chair suggests force. He had been receiving manuscript pages from "Diabelli" via encrypted email and building a systematic decryption. His final email threatened to go to the police. His last call was to Reinhardt. His note on the manuscript: "Eroica ms. -- provenance unverified. Connection to B's Heiligenstadt letter? See K. re: frequency analysis."
- **Kaspar Reinhardt**: Musicologist at the Universitat fur Musik. Early forties, lean, dark hair, sharp concave face. Brilliant and obsessive. Denied tenure twice for his theories about intentional numerical encodings in Beethoven's works -- theories the academic consensus calls pareidolic. His wife left him two years ago (he barely noticed). Drinks like a monk -- as fuel for concentration. He independently mapped forty-seven anomalous intervals in the Eroica's first two hundred bars. He decoded the cipher's warning: "The next one is the one who reads the score" -- making himself the prophesied next victim. He does not want protection; he wants to read the entire score, whatever the cost.
- **Dr. Margarethe Voss**: Musicologist at the Nationalbibliothek. Early fifties, steel-gray close-cropped hair, permanent squint from thirty years of manuscript work. The foremost authority on Beethoven's handwriting. She confirmed the manuscript annotations as Beethoven's hand. She identified Reinhardt as "K." and revealed their friendship (arguing for hours at Cafe Central). Honest but cautious; she understands the implications and is frightened by them.
- **Frau Lindner**: Department secretary at the university. Small, nervous, sixty-something. She described the mysterious visitor -- tall, dark coat of good quality, round silver-framed glasses, a gold lyre pin on his lapel, Northern German accent, carried an architect's portfolio. She also revealed that Zerndl had been asking about security camera gaps.
- **"Diabelli"**: The anonymous figure communicating via ProtonMail. Named after Beethoven's music publisher (Diabelli Variations). Sent Zerndl manuscript pages, then threatened him: "Then you have chosen your zero." Sent Lena the messages ZERNDL=0 and DIE PARTITUR ZEIGT DEN WEG. Connected to or possibly leading the "Bruderschaft der Partitur" (Brotherhood of the Score). Not a scholar -- someone who threatens with annihilation.
- **The visitor with the lyre pin**: Tall man with Northern German accent, round silver glasses, gold lyre lapel pin, architect's portfolio. Visited Zerndl's office the day before his disappearance. Likely an operative of the Brotherhood.

## Story Rules
- **Genre**: Literary thriller with art-history mystery. The investigation operates on two parallel tracks -- criminal (who took Zerndl?) and intellectual (what does the cipher say?). Both tracks must advance together; neither should dominate.
- **Narrative voice**: Third-person limited, closely following Lena. The prose is elegant, measured, and rich with cultural detail. Vienna itself is a character -- its architecture, its music, its institutional habit of secrecy. Metaphors tend toward music, mathematics, and concealment.
- **Music as code**: The central conceit is steganography in music -- messages hidden in the intervals between notes, readable only with the correct numerical key. The cipher is polyalphabetic or positional, not simple substitution. The music itself sounds correct when played; the hidden layer is invisible to listeners. This system must be treated with intellectual rigor.
- **The cipher's self-awareness**: The encoded messages include prophecies about their own decryption ("The next one is the one who reads the score"). This creates a trap-function quality -- reading the cipher activates its consequences. Future chapters should maintain this recursive, self-referential quality.
- **Layered time**: The story constantly moves between present investigation and historical context (Beethoven's era). This should continue -- each discovery in the present should illuminate a historical layer, and vice versa.
- **The Brotherhood of the Score**: Not a theatrical secret society but a "continuity" -- a purpose that outlives its originators. They have guarded something for two hundred years. They operate with institutional patience and are willing to use violence. They should feel ancient, competent, and impersonal.
- **Avoid**: Do not reduce the musical/mathematical elements to window dressing -- they must be integral to the plot. Do not make the Brotherhood cartoonishly evil; they believe they are protecting something. Do not abandon Vienna's specific cultural texture. Do not let Reinhardt die too early -- his obsession and willingness to sacrifice himself is a key dramatic engine. Do not resolve the Beethoven cipher in a single revelation; it should unfold in layers across movements.

## Plot Direction
- **The Karlskirche crypt**: The decoded fragment says "the way leads under the church, where the dead keep their secrets." The Karlskirche, in Beethoven's neighborhood during the Eroica composition, has a crypt. This is the most immediate investigative lead.
- **Reinhardt's safety**: Someone entered his apartment using Zerndl's stolen keys. He is now in a safe house, but he is the kind of man who will not stay put. Protecting him while leveraging his expertise is Lena's central operational challenge.
- **Four movements, four layers**: If each movement of the Eroica contains a steganographic layer, there is an entire hidden document. The first movement yielded a location (the church). The second movement (Marcia funebre -- funeral march) was played live by the unseen violinist as a threat/message. What do the third and fourth movements contain?
- **Zerndl alive or dead?**: ZERNDL=0 could mean he has been killed, or it could mean he has been "zeroed out" -- erased, disappeared, rendered nonexistent. The distinction matters.
- **Diabelli's identity**: The ProtonMail correspondent who provided manuscript pages and then threatened Zerndl. Are they a member of the Brotherhood, or someone trying to crack the Brotherhood's secrets from outside?
- **The lyre-pin visitor**: His identity and role within the Brotherhood. The gold lyre as a recurring symbol to track.
- **What is hidden under the church?**: The ultimate question. Something Beethoven (or his contemporaries) considered worth encoding in one of the greatest compositions in history. Something the Brotherhood has guarded for two centuries. Something people are willing to kidnap and kill over today.
- **Lena's BVT past**: Her three undiscussed years in domestic intelligence gave her the skills for this case but may also create complications -- former colleagues, institutional debts, knowledge she wishes she didn't have.
