Chapter 1: Inheriting the Estate

Something was wrong with the envelope before Elise Hartmann opened it. It smelled of wet earth and sweetness, the way a grave smells when the soil is fresh and the flowers have not yet wilted. She held it to her face and breathed in, and the scent reached into her chest and pulled, and for a moment she was nowhere -- not in her rented London flat, not anywhere she could name. She was in a place that smelled like this. She had never been there. She knew it already.

Inside: a letter from a solicitor's office in Kuala Lumpur. Her great-uncle, Willem Hartmann, had died without children, without a wife, without friends. The funeral had been attended by "estate staff and local representatives." He had lived alone on a rubber plantation in the state of Perak for forty-three years. The estate was called Kebun Suria. Elise had not known he existed.

She was twenty-nine, freelancing as a translator -- Dutch, German, Malay, languages she had collected the way some people collect keys -- and her savings were measured in weeks. She told herself she went because of the money. The buildings were described with legal euphemisms that meant ruin -- "requiring significant restoration," "of historical character." But the truth was that the smell of the envelope had done something to her. It had opened a door in a wall she hadn't known was there, and the air coming through that door was warm and green and alive.

The drive from Ipoh took two hours. Her taxi driver, a Tamil man named Rajan, grew quieter the further they went from town. Oil palms gave way to older growth -- rubber trees planted in colonial rows that had long since lost their discipline, leaning into each other, canopy closing overhead until the car moved through a tunnel of grey-green light. Tapping cups still clung to some trunks, stained black with old latex, like wounds that had scabbed and been forgotten.

"This is Hartmann land?" Rajan asked. Something in his voice she couldn't name.

"I think so. The solicitor said the estate begins at a stone marker."

They had passed it already. She hadn't seen it. Rajan had.

The house appeared without warning -- one moment trees, then the trees opened and there it was. A colonial bungalow raised on pillars, verandahs wrapping its sides like arms folded across a chest. Painted white once; now the color of old teeth, streaked green where moisture had found channels in the plaster. A fig tree had rooted in the gutter, its aerial roots hanging down the wall like fingers reaching for the ground.

It was beautiful. That was the thing she had not expected. Beneath the decay, beneath the vines threading through the balustrade and the shutters hanging crooked, the bones of the house were graceful. Verandah columns turned from local hardwood, dark as coffee. Front stairs sweeping upward in a curve that suggested welcome. Above the door, a fanlight of colored glass -- amber, green, pale blue -- threw soft geometry across the entrance hall.

Rajan set her bags at the foot of the stairs with the care of a man placing something fragile in a location he does not trust.

"There is a hotel in Gopeng. Twenty minutes."

"I'll be fine."

He gave her his card. She watched him drive away faster than she thought necessary.

Inside, the coolness should have been a relief but wasn't. It had a quality -- damp, mineral, like the air inside a well. The checkered tile floor, black and white, had cracked in places, revealing dark earth beneath. To the left, a sitting room with rattan furniture under dust cloths. To the right, a dining room with a table for twelve, its surface fogged with condensation or neglect. A ceiling fan hung motionless, one blade wrapped in cobweb so thick it looked deliberate.

Elise stood in the hallway and listened. The house was not quiet -- no house in the tropics is quiet. The tick and settle of wood in the heat. A beam groaning somewhere above her. Outside, cicadas in a wall of sound, a bird repeating a three-note call like a question never answered. And beneath it all, lower, almost below hearing: a hum. She thought it might be insects in the walls.

She would think this for some time.

The ground floor held a kitchen with a wood-burning stove and a gas range two decades old, a pantry with empty shelves and glass jars of dark sediment, servants' quarters with iron bed frames, a bathroom where the showerhead entered through the wall at an angle that suggested afterthought. The staircase to the upper floor was wide, its banister carved with leaves and fruit she didn't recognize. Halfway up she stopped. A smell -- not the envelope's green mildew but something sweeter and heavier. Frangipani, perhaps, but with an undertone of something organic. She thought rot, then corrected herself: ripeness. The smell of the jungle's patient chemistry turning everything back to earth.

The upper floor had four bedrooms and a locked study. The master bedroom faced east, overlooking the remains of a garden -- skeleton of a pergola strangled by bougainvillea, and beyond it, rubber trees in rows extending into green darkness. On the bedside table: reading glasses and a Malay-English dictionary, open to a page in the M's. Her great-uncle's glasses. His last word looked up.

She picked up the dictionary. She didn't need it -- Malay was one of her languages, picked up during a translation residency in Penang three years ago -- but she looked anyway. The page was open to "mimpi." Dream. She set it down carefully, as though the word might spill.

She chose a smaller room at the back, facing the jungle. A single bed with a mattress that seemed newer than the others, as though someone had anticipated an arrival. The window had no glass -- only wooden shutters she opened to the evening air. The jungle was close here, the undergrowth pressing against what had once been a cleared yard. The clearing was shrinking. Year by year, the jungle was taking back what had been taken from it.

She ate crackers and cheese from Ipoh. The equatorial dusk lasted minutes -- amber to purple to black, and the jungle sounds changed register. Daytime insects yielded to nighttime ones, deeper, more rhythmic. Frogs. Something larger moving in the undergrowth, a single heavy footfall, then silence.

She got into bed and pulled the mosquito net closed and lay listening. The hum was louder now, or more noticeable without her own noise to mask it. It wasn't insects. She was almost sure. It rose and fell in a pattern too regular for biology, too slow for machinery.

It sounded, she thought just before sleep took her, like breathing.

She dreamed of rubber trees bleeding white sap from cuts in their bark, the sap pooling on the ground in a pattern she almost recognized. Someone stood at the edge of the grove watching her. She could not see their face. She did not try.

She woke once in the night. The shutters were closed. She had left them open.
