Chapter 2: The Servants' Taboos

Pak Osman arrived at seven, though Elise had been awake since five -- driven from sleep by the heat and the roosters and by the shutters, which were open again. She was certain she had not opened them in the night. She was certain of very little else.

He came on a motorbike, a small man in his sixties with a face like carved teak and hands that never stopped moving -- touching his chin, adjusting his songkok, smoothing the front of his baju melayu. With him came Mak Su, who would cook and clean. Mak Su was seventy or older, white hair pulled tight, eyes that took in everything and gave back nothing. She carried a basket covered with cloth and went directly to the kitchen without greeting Elise, as though resuming a task that had merely been paused.

"She doesn't speak English," Pak Osman said. Elise had the feeling this was not entirely true.

They sat on the verandah. Pak Osman laid a ring of iron keys on the table -- old, heavy, the kind that looked capable of locking away centuries. He identified each one: front door, back door, pantry, servants' quarters, study, storehouse. The last key he held up separately.

"This is for the room at the end of the upstairs hall. The room you must not open."

His hands, for once, were still.

"Which room?"

"The last room. Left side, at the end." He set the key apart from the others. "Tuan Willem kept it locked. Always locked. He would want me to tell you this."

"What's in it?"

"I don't know. I have never opened it."

There was a formality to the way he said it -- a recitation. As though he had delivered these words before, to someone else, in the same careful tone.

"Pak Osman, I've inherited this estate. I'll need to see every room."

"Yes," he said, and the word contained no agreement.

She let it go. He walked her through the property: the storehouse where rubber sheets had once been smoked, the workers' longhouse now soft with termites and weather, the processing shed with its rusting mangles. The well, stone-lined, with a wooden cover Pak Osman checked each morning.

"Why do you check it?"

He looked at her with patient puzzlement. "Things fall in."

Then the rules. He delivered them as facts -- the way one explains that fire burns or rivers flow downhill.

First: Do not go into the rubber grove after dark. Tamil tappers came before dawn from a village three miles east, every third day. No one else entered at night.

"Snakes?" Elise asked.

"Snakes, yes. Other things."

He did not elaborate.

Second: Do not leave food uncovered after sunset. Anything on a plate or counter must be covered or removed before the light failed. This included water. Especially water.

"Water?"

"Still water must be covered." He touched his chin. "Reflections."

The word sat between them. He did not explain it.

Third: If she heard knocking at night, she must not answer. Not open any door, not go to any window, not call out. She was to stay in bed, close her eyes, and wait. It would stop. It always stopped.

"This is a house," Elise said, her voice taking on the careful quality of someone managing an unreasonable situation. "Wood expands and contracts --"

"Yes," he said. "But this is not wood."

He said it simply, with the gentle immovability of someone who has outlasted many arguments on this subject.

Fourth: She could enter the study -- the key was on the ring -- but she must not remove any of Willem's papers. She could read them, not take them from the room. His hands moved faster when she pressed.

"They are his work. Many years. He said the papers must stay together, in order."

"What kind of work?"

"He studied the history of this place. Before the plantation, before the British. What was here before." His hands went to his chin. "He found things."

Mak Su appeared with coffee and kuih -- rice flour and coconut cakes, pale green and white, arranged in a pattern that might have been decorative or something else. She set the plate down and looked at Elise for the first time. Her eyes were dark and flat and appraising.

She spoke in Malay. Elise understood every word -- Mak Su had said, "She has his eyes. She will stay too long, like he did" -- but before she could respond, Pak Osman translated: "She says welcome to Kebun Suria. She says you look like your uncle."

Elise was fair and light-skinned. Willem, in the single photograph the solicitor had provided, was dark and weathered as a boat hull. The translation was wrong, and Pak Osman knew she spoke Malay. He had seen the dictionary upstairs. She did not point any of this out.

After breakfast, Pak Osman showed her the study. Smaller than expected, lined floor to ceiling with shelves. Books in English, Dutch, Malay -- three of her four working languages -- and a fourth she couldn't identify, angular script that might have been Javanese or something older, something the alphabet had forgotten. Dozens of marbled-cover notebooks in Willem's handwriting, switching between languages mid-sentence, as though the thing he was trying to describe could only be approached from multiple directions at once. Hand-drawn maps on tracing paper. Photographs pinned to a corkboard: the estate across decades, close-ups of stone carvings half-swallowed by roots.

On the desk, weighed down by a river stone, a single sheet:

TO WHOEVER COMES AFTER ME:
The house is not the problem. The house is a symptom. What is underneath has been here longer than the trees, longer than the people, longer than the name anyone living remembers. I have spent forty years trying to understand it. I have failed. Follow the rules. Keep the room locked. Cover the water. Do not answer the knocking.
If you cannot do these things, leave. The land does not need us. It endures.
-- W.H.

Elise read it twice. "He was eccentric," she said. It was not a question, but she wanted it to be.

"He was careful," Pak Osman replied.

She spent the afternoon reading the notebooks. Willem had traced the estate's history to its founding in 1898 by a Dutch planter named Jansen, and before that to the Malay kampung on the site, and before that to something else -- references to pre-Malay settlements, stone foundations found beneath topsoil during drainage work, carvings matching no known tradition. He had written to archaeologists at the University of Malaya. Their replies were polite, noncommittal. One, from 1997, noted: "The motifs you describe do not correspond to any catalogued tradition in the peninsula. We would advise caution in interpreting surface finds without professional excavation."

They had not come. No one had come. Willem had continued alone.

At five, Mak Su brought tea and nasi lemak in banana leaf. She set a glass of water on the desk, then paused and placed a saucer over it. She caught Elise watching and held her gaze -- not hostile, not warm, but communicating something precise.

Elise waited until Mak Su left. She removed the saucer and looked at the water. Her face reflected in it, distorted by the curve of the glass, and for a fraction of a second the reflection did something her face did not do.

It smiled.

She put the saucer back.

Pak Osman left at six. Mak Su had already gone, her motorbike puttering down the estate road. Elise stood on the verandah and watched the rubber trees swallow the last daylight, their rows converging into shadow like lines drawn toward a vanishing point she could not see.

She went inside. She covered the water in the kitchen. She checked the doors. She climbed the stairs.

At the end of the hall, the locked room waited. The door was darker than the others -- or perhaps it had absorbed more of whatever darkness the house had to offer. She touched the handle. It was cold. Not cool, as things in shade are cool. Cold, aggressively, in defiance of the equatorial night pressing against the rest of the house.

She did not try the key. Not tonight.

She lay down and listened to the jungle and the house and the space between them where something hummed its low, patient, endless note.

At half past midnight, the knocking began.
