# Rules: The Plantation House

## Setting
- **Time**: Present day (recent), with historical layers stretching back to 1898 (Dutch colonial founding), pre-Malay settlements, and something far older
- **Place**: Kebun Suria, a rubber plantation estate in Perak, Malaysia, roughly two hours from Ipoh. A colonial bungalow raised on pillars, surrounded by undisciplined rubber trees that have lost their colonial rows
- **Key locations**: The main house (verandah, entrance hall with checkered tiles, kitchen, servants' quarters, upper floor with four bedrooms, Willem's study, and the locked room at the end of the hall); the rubber grove; the well; the workers' longhouse (termite-softened); the processing shed
- **Atmosphere**: Equatorial heat, relentless moisture, jungle pressing inward. The estate is being reclaimed by nature. Everything is described through sensory texture -- smells (frangipani and rot, deep earth, latex), sounds (the hum, cicadas, knocking), touch (cold door handles in tropical heat). The horror is patient, geological, ancient
- **Social context**: Post-colonial Malaysia; the estate exists in a liminal space between British rubber economy legacy, Malay spiritual tradition, and something pre-human

## Characters
- **Elise Hartmann** (29, female): Freelance translator (Dutch, German, Malay, English). Inherits Kebun Suria from great-uncle Willem, whom she never knew. Practical, linguistically gifted, financially precarious. She understands Malay but strategically conceals this. Her defining trait is a refusal to leave until she understands -- intellectual stubbornness that borders on recklessness. She is drawn to the estate by something she cannot explain (the smell of the envelope)
- **Pak Osman** (60s, male): Estate caretaker, a small man with restless hands. He delivers the house rules with the immovability of natural law. His family has served the estate for generations (his father worked for the Jansen family). He knows more than he says, communicates through omission and gesture. He is loyal to Willem's memory and genuinely protective of Elise, but his protection takes the form of rules rather than explanations
- **Mak Su** (70+, female): Cook and cleaner. White-haired, flat-eyed, assessing. Claims not to speak English (likely untrue). She has served the house for decades and performs daily rituals (covering water, mopping wet footprints) with the efficiency of long practice. Her singing is protective. She recognized something in Elise immediately ("She has his eyes. She will stay too long")
- **Willem Hartmann** (deceased): Elise's great-uncle. Lived alone on the estate for 43 years, studying what lies beneath the land. His study is filled with multilingual notebooks, hand-drawn maps, and photographs of stone carvings. He found a carved stone in 1980-81 during drainage work and placed it in the locked room, after which the knocking changed. His final note warns whoever comes after him. He failed to understand it; he followed the rules
- **Rajan**: Tamil taxi driver who drove Elise from Ipoh. Recognized the Hartmann land before Elise did, drove away faster than necessary
- **The entity/presence**: Not a ghost. Something that occupies the land the way water occupies a river. Pre-Malay, pre-human, connected to the carved stone. It manifests through: the hum (constant, below hearing), nightly knocking (three knocks, circling the house), warm water footprints on tiles, closed/opened shutters, the carved stone's sweating white fluid, and the face that assembles in the stone's channels

## Story Rules
- **Genre**: Tropical Gothic horror with colonial-era archaeology undertones. Slow burn, literary register
- **Narrative voice**: Third person limited (Elise's perspective). Prose is precise, sensory-rich, with long sentences that mirror the patient, accumulating dread. Observations are layered -- what Elise sees, what she thinks she sees, what she refuses to see
- **The rules system**: The story is structured around taboos delivered by Pak Osman: (1) Don't enter the rubber grove after dark, (2) Cover all still water after sunset (reflections), (3) Don't answer nighttime knocking, (4) Don't remove papers from the study, (5) Don't open the locked room. Each rule exists to be tested. Breaking a rule produces consequences that escalate the horror
- **The knocking**: Always three knocks, always circles the house, always at night. After the stone was brought inside, knocking sometimes comes from inside too. The knocking is mechanical, precise -- it wants to be recognized as a pattern
- **Water and fluid**: Central motif. The stone sweats white latex-like fluid. Warm water appears on tiles in walking patterns. Still water shows wrong reflections. Moisture migrates through carved channels. The entity's medium seems to be liquid
- **Translation as theme**: Elise is a translator; the entire story is about translation -- between languages, between worlds, between what the land remembers and what humans can understand. Willem's notebooks switch languages mid-sentence because the truth can only be approached from multiple directions
- **Colonial guilt**: The plantation is built on extraction (rubber, land, labor). The entity predates colonialism but the colonial infrastructure disturbed it. The horror has layers of historical violence beneath the supernatural
- **Pacing**: Each chapter covers roughly one day. Horror intensifies by the chapter. Chapter 1 = arrival and the hum. Chapter 2 = the rules and the first knocking. Chapter 3 = the locked room and the stone
- **Avoid**: Jump scares, gore, exorcism, easy explanations. The entity should never be fully understood. Do not reduce it to a "vengeful spirit" -- it is something older and stranger. Do not have characters behave stupidly for plot convenience; Elise is smart and still chooses to stay

## Plot Direction
- **The stone**: The carved stone is the focal point. Its channels move white fluid in patterns that sometimes form a face -- not human, proportions wrong. It pulses like a heartbeat. The sap is now seeping under the locked door. What happens when it reaches the rest of the house?
- **Willem's research**: His notebooks contain decades of investigation into pre-Malay stone foundations, unidentifiable carvings, and something beneath the estate. University archaeologists declined to investigate. What did Willem ultimately discover? Did he die of natural causes?
- **The locked room**: Elise opened it and saw the stone. She locked it again. But the sap is coming under the door. The room's darkness has density and resists light. Will she return?
- **Mak Su's knowledge**: She knows far more than she reveals. Her singing is protective. Her immediate recognition of Elise suggests prophecy or pattern. What is her role?
- **The well**: Pak Osman checks it every morning because "things fall in." What has fallen in? What might come out?
- **The reflection**: Elise's reflection smiled when she didn't. Still water is dangerous in this house. What do the reflections show?
- **The bomoh**: Pak Osman mentioned a bomoh (Malay shaman) from the old kampung. Is this person still alive? Could they help?
- **Night escalation**: The knocking circled outside, then started inside after the stone arrived. What is the next stage? Will it stop knocking and start doing something else?
- **Elise's connection**: The envelope's smell called to her. She chose the back bedroom as if anticipated. Willem's dictionary was open to "mimpi" (dream). Is she connected to this place in ways beyond inheritance?
