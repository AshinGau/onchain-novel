Chapter 3: The First Night

It began at the front door. Three knocks, evenly spaced, the interval between them precise enough to suggest a clock or a heartbeat or something that had learned to imitate both. Elise lay in bed and told herself: wood. Thermal contraction. A branch. The explanations assembled themselves with the desperate efficiency of sandbags against a rising flood.

The knocking stopped. The silence that followed was not relief -- it was the held breath before the next sentence in a conversation she had not agreed to have.

Then it came again. At the back door. The same three knocks, the same spacing. As though whatever stood outside had walked around the house. As though it wanted her to know it could.

She did not get up. Pak Osman's rule held her in the bed like a physical weight. Close your eyes. She closed them. Behind her eyelids the darkness moved, organized itself into shapes that dissolved when she tried to focus, like ink dropped in water.

The knocking circled the house. Dining room windows. Kitchen door. Servants' quarters. Each set identical -- three knocks, mechanically precise, as though variation was not the point. The point was the pattern. The point was that she recognize it.

When it reached the wall beneath her window, it stopped.

The temperature in the room dropped. Not gradually, the way night air cools -- suddenly, as though something cold and vast had pressed itself against the outside of the house and the wall could not hold the warmth. The sweet smell from the staircase returned, stronger now: frangipani and rot, ripeness tipping into decay.

She lay with her eyes closed and her breath shallow and waited. The hum was louder now -- inside the room, inside her chest, vibrating against her ribs like a second heartbeat pressed against her own.

A sound at the window. Not knocking. Softer, more intimate. A palm pressed flat against wood, sliding slowly. The shutters flexed inward, bowing under patient, distributed weight. Something leaned against them the way you lean against a door to feel if there's heat on the other side.

Elise bit the inside of her cheek. The pain was clarifying. She was awake. This was happening.

The pressure released. She heard something move along the outside wall. Upward. A continuous dragging sound, like cloth over plaster. It went up the wall to the roof. She heard it cross overhead, terracotta tiles clicking faintly under a weight that was somehow both heavy and careful.

Then nothing. The jungle sounds resumed as though someone had restored the volume. Crickets, frogs, a nightjar. The hum subsided, or she stopped distinguishing it from her own pulse.

She did not sleep. She lay awake until the roosters crowed and grey light seeped through the shutters -- closed, she confirmed, exactly as she had left them -- and the house settled into its daytime self, which was merely old and damp and falling apart.

She went downstairs at first light. The entrance hall tiles were wet. Not uniformly -- wet in a line from the front door to the base of the staircase, as though something had walked that path. Something that carried water, or was made of it. She knelt and touched the mark. The water was warm. Body temperature.

She stood and went to the kitchen and made coffee and drank it facing the wall, not thinking about the water on the tiles, thinking about the water on the tiles.

Mak Su arrived first, came in through the back door without knocking -- Elise flinched, then felt foolish -- and went to the entrance hall and stood looking at the wet marks. She said nothing. She got a mop. She cleaned them with the quick motions of someone who has done this many times.

Pak Osman arrived twenty minutes later. He looked at Elise's face and understood. He did not ask what had happened. He asked: "Did you open anything?"

"No."

"Did you answer?"

"No."

He nodded -- approval or relief or the weariness of a man who has had this conversation before.

"I need you to tell me what this is."

He sat on the verandah floor, cross-legged, as though what he was about to say required a different posture.

"I cannot tell you what it is. Tuan Willem could not. I can tell you what the old people said." He touched his chin. "My grandmother, the bomoh who lived in the kampung before the kampung moved -- they said this land is occupied. Not haunted. Occupied. The way a house is occupied when someone lives in it. Something lives in the land. Not under it. In it. The way water lives in a river."

"And the knocking?"

"Since before I was born. My father worked for the Jansen family. He heard it. His father heard it. Tuan Willem heard it every night for forty-three years."

"Every night?"

"Every night."

Forty-three years. Every night the same three knocks circling the house, and inside, a man alone, eyes closed, following the rules. And in the morning the water on the tiles and Mak Su with her mop, and the day beginning as though the night had been a dream, except that it wasn't, except that the water was real and warm and patterned on the floor.

"The locked room," Elise said. "What's in it?"

Pak Osman's hands stopped. Completely still. He looked at her with something she hadn't seen before -- not patience, not puzzlement, but something older, living behind his careful courtesy like a shape behind a curtain.

"Tuan Willem found something in the ground. When he dug the new drainage, nineteen-eighty, maybe eighty-one. Carved stone. Very old. He brought it inside, put it in that room." Pak Osman paused. "After that, the knocking changed."

"Changed how?"

"Before, it knocked on the outside." His hands began to move again, as though they had been holding their breath. "After he brought the stone in -- sometimes it knocked on the inside too."

The morning was bright and loud with birds and Mak Su was washing dishes in the kitchen and these things were solid and real and obedient to every law Elise had ever trusted.

"I want to see the room," she said.

"I know," said Pak Osman. And then, quietly, as though he did not want the house to hear: "He wanted to see it too."

She went upstairs. The hallway was lit by a window at the far end, light falling in a long rectangle on the floorboards, and where the light met the shadow, the locked door waited. She took the small key from her pocket. She had separated it from the others instinctively, the way you keep a sick animal apart from the herd.

The handle was cold enough to make her breath catch. Ten in the morning. Thirty-three degrees outside.

She turned the key. The door opened inward, into darkness that should not have been possible. The room had a window -- she had seen it from outside. But the darkness was complete, fundamental, as though light was not something the room had ever agreed to admit.

She turned on her phone's flashlight. The beam entered the room and seemed to stop -- not that the space was vast, but that the darkness had density, texture, resistance. She could see the floor: bare boards, stained dark. The near wall: cracked plaster with that same darkening. And in the center of the room, the edge of something.

She stepped inside. The air was cold and wet and smelled of deep earth -- not garden soil but the kind you reach only past roots and clay and stone, earth that has never seen the sun.

The stone was roughly oval, a meter long, half a meter wide, dark grey. Smooth in places, carved in others. The carvings were not decorative and not writing -- they were channels cut into the surface in patterns that curved and intersected and doubled back on themselves. Some channels were deeper than others. Some of them were wet.

The stone was sweating. Moisture beaded on its surface and collected in the carved grooves, and the moisture moved. Not dripping. Migrating. Slowly, from one groove to the next, following the channels as though they were not decoration but infrastructure -- a system designed to move something from one place to another.

She bent closer. The moisture was not clear. It was white. Translucent, pale -- the color of latex. The color of rubber sap. The color of what came out of the trees when you cut them and let them bleed.

Her phone light flickered. Not dimmed -- flickered, as though something had passed between the light and the stone. Nothing had moved. She was alone.

In the flicker, she saw the carvings differently. For a fraction of a second, the grooves resolved into a shape. A face. Not human -- proportions wrong, features too wide, too flat, arranged with a geometry no human skull could support. But a face. Looking up from the stone's surface, assembled from carved channels and the white fluid that moved through them like blood through veins, like sap through a scored tree.

The moisture pulsed. All of it, every channel, every groove, at once. Like a heartbeat. Like the hum she had heard since she arrived, made visible.

Elise stepped back. Her heel caught the door frame. She pulled the door shut and locked it. Her hands shook but the key found the lock on the first try, as though the lock wanted to be closed, as though the house was helping her do what she should have done from the beginning.

She leaned against the wall. Her breath came hard. The hallway was warm. The light from the window touched her face.

Downstairs, Mak Su began to sing -- a Malay song, low and tuneless, the kind you sing not for beauty but for protection. The melody wound through the house like smoke, and Elise listened and felt the stone's pulse still in her fingertips and knew she would not leave. Not yet. Not until she understood what Willem had found in the ground and what it was doing to this house and whether the knocking that came every night was something trying to get in, or something trying to get out.

She looked at the locked door. In the gap between the door and the floor, in the thin line of darkness, something moved. White. Slow. Patient.

The sap was coming under the door.
