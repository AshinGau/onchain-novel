Chapter 1: The Blood of Oleanders

They buried Petru Ferracci on a Thursday, and the oleanders along the cemetery wall were in full, poisonous bloom.

The village of Cozzano sits in the granite fold of Corsica's interior, where the mountains still hold snow in April and the chestnut forests run so thick that a man can vanish into them and never be found. The church of San Ghjuvanni is eight hundred years old, its bell cracked since Napoleon's time, and it rang now with that distinctive fractured note -- not a toll but a complaint, as though the bell itself objected to the frequency with which it was asked to mourn.

Anto Ferracci stood at the head of the coffin and did not weep. He was thirty-one years old, with the dark, angular face common to the mountain Corsicans -- cheekbones like ledges, deep-set eyes the color of wet slate. He had a habit, when thinking, of running his thumb along the callus on his left index finger, a ridge of hard skin built up from years of drawing a bowstring -- he had been an archer in his youth, competitive enough to represent the department, before his father had told him to put away the bow and learn the gun. He was doing it now, thumb against callus, a small repetitive motion that the people around him mistook for grief. It was not grief. It was calculation.

He wore a suit that had been his father's, black wool too heavy for the season, the sweat collecting along his collarbone without his noticing.

Around him, the village had gathered -- not out of affection for the dead man, who had been difficult, but out of respect for the ritual. In Corsica, you attend funerals. It is not a choice. The women wore black from throat to ankle, many of them wearing it for years, for other deaths, and would continue until their own. The men stood in clusters by the cemetery gate, caps removed, speaking in low Corsican -- not French, never French for the dead.

Petru Ferracci had been sixty-seven. A shepherd who had also been something more, in the manner of Corsican men who live by the old codes. He had carried a Beretta in his waistband for forty years and had used it twice: once to kill a dog that had gone after his flock, and once, in 1981, to put a bullet through the thigh of a man named Santu Lucchesi. The thigh shot had been deliberate -- a warning, not a murder. In the grammar of vendetta, it was a semicolon, not a period.

But the Lucchesis had not read it that way.

Three weeks before the funeral, Petru Ferracci had been found in his bergerie -- the stone shepherd's hut high in the maquis above Cozzano -- with his throat cut from ear to ear. His flock was still penned. His rifle hung untouched on the wall. His dog, a gray Corsican sheepdog named Orsu, lay beside him with its skull crushed by a single blow. Whoever had come for Petru had come while he slept, had dealt with the dog first, and had used a knife rather than a gun. The knife was a message. Guns are modern, impersonal, efficient. A knife across the throat, in the old language, says: I was close enough to smell your breath, and I chose this.

The gendarmes from Ajaccio had come, taken photographs, asked questions that no one answered, and left. This was expected. The gendarmes were French. The matter was Corsican.

Anto watched the coffin descend. The ropes creaked. The priest, Father Benedettu -- who had baptized Anto, married Anto's parents, and would, if the mathematics of vendetta held, likely bury more Ferraccis before his own time came -- spoke the words in Latin and then again in Corsican. The Latin was for God. The Corsican was for the dead.

His mother stood beside him. Livia Ferracci, born Livia Sansonetti of Bastelica, a woman of fifty-eight who carried grief not in her face but in her spine -- she had always been tall, and she was taller now, as though sorrow had pulled her upward instead of down. She had not spoken since they brought the body from the bergerie. Not a word. The other women understood. There is a stage of mourning in Corsica that precedes the voceru -- the death lament -- and it is silence. The silence is not peace. It is the gathering of breath.

After the burial, the family returned to the house. The Ferracci home was stone and chestnut timber, three stories, built into the slope so that you entered the top floor from the road and descended into the kitchen, which opened onto a terrace overlooking the valley. Food had been laid out by the neighbors: chestnut polenta, figatellu sausage, brocciu cheese, bread baked in a wood oven that had not been cold in a hundred years. Wine from the Ferracci vineyard -- a small plot on the south-facing slope, producing a rough red that no one outside the valley would drink but that tasted, to anyone raised on it, like the definition of home.

Anto ate nothing. He sat at the long table while the house filled with relatives and neighbors, their voices a low hum through the stone rooms. His cousin Marcu, a mechanic in Ajaccio who had driven up in a battered Peugeot that smelled of brake fluid, sat across from him and poured two glasses of wine without asking.

"Drink," Marcu said.

Anto drank. The wine was the color of old blood.

"I know who it was," Anto said. He said it in Corsican, quietly, with the flatness of a man stating the temperature.

Marcu looked at him but did not ask. You do not ask a man to name his enemy in a room full of people. Walls listen. Even family walls.

Later, when the house had emptied, Anto walked onto the terrace. The valley below was dark, the chestnut trees black against a sky salted with stars. A Eurasian scops owl called from the maquis -- a single note, patient as a metronome. The air smelled of rosemary and stone dust and the particular sweetness of early summer scrubland, that blend of cistus and myrtle and wild mint that the French call garrigue but that Corsicans simply call home.

He did not hear his mother come onto the terrace. She moved without sound, a skill he had always attributed to the granite floors but which he now understood was deliberate.

"Do not do what you are thinking of doing," Livia said. Her voice was hoarse from disuse, cracked as the church bell.

"Mama --"

"I know what you are thinking because your father thought it first, thirty years ago, and it cost us everything. The vendetta took his sleep, then his laughter, then his friends, and finally his life. It is a fire that warms nothing."

Anto looked at her. In the starlight, her face was all angles and shadow, a face carved by the same geography that had carved the mountains.

"And if I do nothing?"

Livia was quiet for a long moment. When she spoke, it was not in French but in the oldest Corsican, the dialect of the interior mountains that even some Corsicans could not follow.

"Then your father dies twice. Once by the knife. And once by the forgetting." She touched his face -- a brief, dry touch, like a leaf against stone. "I am not telling you to forget. I am telling you to survive."

She went inside. Anto stood alone on the terrace and understood, with the clarity that comes after midnight, that his mother had not forbidden the vendetta. She had given it a condition: come back alive.

He took his father's Beretta from the kitchen drawer. It was a 92FS, worn smooth at the grip, the bluing rubbed to silver along the barrel. He checked the magazine, racked the slide, set the safety. These were motions his father had taught him at fourteen, in the chestnut grove behind the bergerie, with the same matter-of-fact instruction used for skinning a boar. This is how you hold it. This is how you breathe. This is how you become still.

He sat on the terrace wall, the pistol on the stone beside him, and spoke to the dark.

"Santu Lucchesi. Or his sons. It does not matter which hand held the knife. The family is the hand."

This was not a vow spoken for drama. There was no audience, no blood oath sworn on a crucifix as in the old stories. The real vendetta was quieter. It was a decision made in the body before the mind could intervene -- not chosen but arrived, like weather, like stone fracturing along a line that was always there.

He sat until the stars wheeled and the owl fell silent and the first gray light appeared over the ridge. Then he went inside, disassembled the Beretta, cleaned it with the oil his father kept in a tin above the sink, reassembled it, and placed it in his waistband at the small of his back.

The weight of it there felt like a second spine.

He had three weeks of leave from his job at the port authority in Bastia. Three weeks in which the world expected him to grieve. He would grieve. But grief, in Corsica, has never been passive. It is a verb. It moves. It has direction and velocity and, at the end of its trajectory, a destination.

He would need to be careful. The Lucchesis were not shepherds. Not anymore.
