Chapter 2: The Empire of the Lucchesis

The Lucchesi family had come from the same mountains as the Ferraccis, from a village called Guitera-les-Bains three valleys to the west, where the hot springs smell of sulfur and the old women say the devil once bathed. But that was two generations ago. The Lucchesis had come down from the mountains.

Santu Lucchesi, the patriarch, was seventy-two years old and had not set foot in Guitera in fifteen years. He lived in a villa on the Route des Sanguinaires, the coastal road south of Ajaccio where the red granite cliffs drop into water so blue it looks artificial. The villa was called A Torra -- The Tower -- though it had no tower. It had a swimming pool, a tennis court that no one used, and a view of the Iles Sanguinaires that Santu described as "adequate." He was a man who found adequacy in most things, which makes a person either a philosopher or dangerous. Santu was not a philosopher.

Anto drove to Ajaccio on the Monday after the funeral. He took the mountain road through Zicavo, the slower route, because it was empty and gave him time to think. His gray Renault Clio had 180,000 kilometers on the clock and a crack in the windshield that had been there so long it felt permanent. He drove with the window down, the maquis-scented air filling the car, running his thumb along that bowstring callus as he turned over what he knew.

What he knew was this: the Lucchesis were no longer a mountain family. They were a Corsican institution.

Santu had three sons. The eldest, Petru-Santu, ran the family's construction company, Batisseurs du Sud -- the largest firm in southern Corsica, holding contracts for roads, public buildings, and the tourist infrastructure swallowing the coast. He was forty-five, served on the municipal council, wore linen suits, and spoke French with the accent of a man educated on the continent. Legitimate, by every external measure.

The second son, Dumé, was forty-one and managed bars, nightclubs, and restaurants in Ajaccio and Porto-Vecchio. Businesses profitable beyond what their customer counts could explain. Dumé was the one newspapers called "controversial" when they mentioned him at all, which was rarely, because Corsican newspapers understood the cost of specificity.

The third son was different. Lisandru Lucchesi was thirty-three and did not exist on paper -- no official employment, no social media, no photographs at public events. What was known about Lisandru, in the way things are known in Corsica without being spoken, was that he handled what his brothers could not. Lisandru was his father's knife.

Anto parked on the Cours Napoleon and walked. Ajaccio smelled of diesel and sea salt and the stale sweetness of a Mediterranean port in summer. He passed Napoleon's birthplace, now a museum, and thought, as he always did, that Corsica had produced one emperor and had been paying for it ever since.

He went to a bar on the Rue Fesch called Chez Matteu. Narrow room, zinc counter, football on the television, and a patron named Matteu Ferracci -- a distant cousin, close enough to trust, distant enough to deny. Matteu was sixty, his face like a dried fig, his hands trembling from Parkinson's. But his memory was architectural. He remembered every transaction, debt, insult, and alliance in Ajaccio going back forty years.

"I need to understand the Lucchesis," Anto said. He ordered a pastis and left it untouched. The glass sat between them on the zinc like a prop in a scene they both understood.

Matteu polished a clean glass. "Which part?"

"The money. Where it comes from. Where it goes. Who depends on them."

"You are asking me to draw a map of a country."

"Then draw it."

Matteu drew it -- not on paper, nothing ever written down, but in quiet, precise Corsican. The Lucchesi empire, he said, was structured like the island itself: a visible coast, and behind it, mountains that no outsider could navigate.

The coast was Batisseurs du Sud. Construction. Real. It built roads and schools and the new hospital wing. It employed three hundred people, most from families owing the Lucchesis favors older than the company. The contracts came from regional government, and regional government included men who owed Santu their seats -- not through bribery, which was crude, continental, but through the Corsican system: a daughter who needed work, a planning application that needed a signature, a brother who needed a charge to disappear. These debts were currency, and Santu had been the island's central bank for thirty years.

Behind the construction: property. Inland parcels, mountainsides, valleys where roads would someday be built or never be built, depending on profit. Rental properties in Ajaccio and Bastia. A marina in Porto-Vecchio. Hotels on the east coast, held through companies registered in Marseille.

And behind the property, in the mountains where no road reached: the rest.

Matteu did not say "drugs." He said "import-export." Cannabis from Morocco by boat on the east coast, distributed through Dume's network and contacts in Marseille. Not a cartel -- the Lucchesis would have been insulted by the comparison. A family business, run with the same pragmatism their grandfathers brought to charcoal and cheese. The product changed. The method had not.

"How much?" Anto asked.

"Enough to buy a regional councilor. Not enough to buy a senator."

"And Lisandru?"

Matteu stopped polishing. The look he gave Anto was diagnostic, a doctor recognizing a symptom he did not like.

"Lisandru is the one you need to worry about. The others are businessmen. Lisandru is something from the old time. Five years in the Foreign Legion -- Second REP, paratroopers, based in Calvi. He left with skills a civilian cannot buy. Not married. No children. No attachments that can be used against him. He moves quietly, and he has his father's patience."

"Patience," Anto repeated.

"Your father shot Santu in the thigh in 1981. Santu waited forty years. That is patience."

Anto drank the pastis. It tasted of anise and kerosene. "My father's dispute with Santu -- what was it about?"

"Land. What else? A boundary between the Ferracci pastures and the Lucchesi forest above Guitera. A chestnut grove both families claimed. Your grandfather and Santu's father agreed on a line. Then someone moved a stone marker. Then goats crossed the line. Then goats were found dead. Then your father put a bullet in Santu's leg."

"Over goats."

"Over principle. The goats were the excuse. The principle was: you do not take what is mine. Once violated, that principle cannot be restored by negotiation. Only by blood."

Anto left and walked along the port. The ferries to Marseille and Nice sat in their berths, white and massive, disgorging tourists who blinked in the Corsican sun. He watched them without seeing them. He was building his own map now -- not of territory but of vulnerability.

The Lucchesi empire was large, but large things have joints. Joints can be broken.

Petru-Santu's company depended on government contracts. Government contracts depended on political relationships. Political relationships depended on discretion.

Dume's network depended on a supply chain that crossed water. Water routes can be interrupted.

And Lisandru depended on being invisible. Invisible men can be made visible.

He drove north toward Bastia, taking the coast road. Near Sagone, where the road curves along cliff edges and the maquis comes down to the asphalt, he pulled over and called Marcu.

"I need you to find someone. Vannina Lucchesi, Petru-Santu's wife. Her routine -- where she goes, who she sees, what she does."

Silence on the line. The Mediterranean was black below the cliffs except where moonlight caught the wave crests.

"Anto," Marcu said carefully. "Women are not part of this."

"I am not going to hurt her. I need to understand her husband through her. A wife knows his schedule, his worries, his weak days. She knows when he drinks and when he cannot sleep. She knows the shape of his fear."

"And if the Lucchesis learn you are watching?"

"Then they will know what they already know. That a Ferracci is still alive."

He hung up and sat while the engine cooled. His mother's words returned: I am not telling you to forget. I am telling you to survive. He turned them over, testing their weight, and found that they did not conflict with what he intended. Survival and vengeance were not opposites. In Corsica, they had always been the same thing.

The stars were bright enough to navigate by, which is what his grandfather had done, running supplies through these mountains during the war. Navigation by stars requires patience, a tolerance for darkness, and the ability to hold a destination in mind long after it has vanished from sight.

Anto had all three.
