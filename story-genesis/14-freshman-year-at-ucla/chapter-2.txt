Bootstrap Chapter 2: The Geometry of Pretending

The party was on Landfair Avenue, in one of those apartment complexes that exists specifically to convert parental tuition money into noise and regret. Desmond had heard about it from a girl in his Intro to African American Studies class, who had heard about it from a boy on the water polo team, who had heard about it from his fraternity brother, who had organized it. This is how information moved at UCLA -- through a chain of attractive strangers, each link polished by social capital I did not possess.

"You're coming," Desmond said. It was not a question. He was standing in front of the mirror we'd hung on the back of our door, adjusting a chain that caught the light. Three weeks into the quarter and he had already built a social architecture I could barely comprehend -- group chats, study sessions that were really flirtation laboratories, a rotation of dining companions who appeared and dissolved according to rules I hadn't learned.

"I have a paper due Monday," I said. This was true. It was also irrelevant. The paper was for my Writing 2 class, a close reading of a Flannery O'Connor story, and I had already written it twice -- once earnestly, with the careful analysis my high school English teacher would have praised, and once again from scratch because I'd read my section-mate's draft and realized that what passed for insight in Bakersfield was remedial here. She had used the word "liminal" three times. I had to Google it.

"It's Thursday," Desmond said. "Paper's due Monday. You've got time."

"I need to revise it."

"You need to live." He turned from the mirror and looked at me with something that wasn't pity, exactly, but its neighboring emotion -- the concern of someone who has diagnosed a problem before the patient knows they're sick. "Elias. My guy. You've been in the library every night this week. You eat alone at Bruin Plate. You haven't talked to anyone on our floor except me and the RA, and the RA doesn't count because she's paid to talk to you."

All of this was accurate and all of it stung. I had been hiding, and the fact that Desmond had noticed meant the hiding wasn't working.

"One hour," I said.

"Two."

"Ninety minutes."

"Deal."

We walked down Veteran Avenue in the warm October dark, past the sorority houses with their columns and their perfect landscaping and their sense of belonging so aggressive it was almost architectural. Greek Row at UCLA is a monument to the idea that privilege, if arranged correctly, can look like tradition. I watched girls in crop tops and platform sneakers streaming toward Gayley, their laughter bright and performative, and I felt the way I always felt around groups -- like a foreign correspondent reporting from a country whose customs I'd studied but never practiced.

The apartment was on the second floor and the bass was already shaking the stairs. Desmond moved through the door with the ease of water finding its level, immediately absorbed into the crowd, fist-bumping a guy I didn't recognize, hugging a girl I'd seen in the dining hall. I stood near the entrance, holding a red cup someone had handed me, and performed the ancient ritual of the introvert at a party: I leaned against the wall and pretended to check my phone.

The apartment was temporary squalor elevated to art form. LED strips on the ceiling cast everything blue-purple. The kitchen counter was a landscape of bottles, and a boy in a tank top was DJing from a laptop balanced on an ironing board. He was terrible, but no one cared -- the music wasn't aesthetic, it was social, a shared language that let strangers move near each other without the burden of conversation.

I sipped from my red cup. Jungle juice -- sweet enough to disguise the alcohol, strong enough to erase inhibitions. I should be careful. I drank more.

A girl materialized next to me. She was short, East Asian, with a streak of silver in her black hair and a tattoo of a crescent moon behind her left ear. She wore a vintage Nirvana t-shirt and the expression of someone who was also performing the role of Casual Person at a Party.

"You look like you're doing math in your head," she said.

"I'm calculating the structural load of the ironing board."

She laughed. It was a real laugh, not the performative kind, and it changed her face completely -- her eyes narrowed, her nose wrinkled, and for a moment she looked like a kid who had been surprised by joy.

"I'm June," she said.

"Elias."

"What's your major?"

"Undeclared. You?"

"Molecular biology. But I'm starting to think that was a cry for help."

I laughed, and the laugh surprised me, because I had not laughed -- genuinely, involuntarily -- since arriving at UCLA. The sound of it was like finding a room in my own house I'd forgotten existed.

We talked. This was the remarkable thing. We simply talked, the way I'd seen other people talk but had convinced myself I was incapable of replicating. June was from San Jose. Her parents were Taiwanese immigrants who owned a boba shop and wanted her to be a doctor, a desire she described as "love expressed in the imperative tense." She had a younger brother who was, she said, the favorite child, and she delivered this information with the cheerful fatalism of someone who had made peace with a fact rather than a wound. She was funny -- actually funny, not the defensive sarcasm I used as a substitute -- and she had a habit of touching the moon tattoo behind her ear when she was thinking, as though consulting a private oracle. She asked questions the way good readers read, with attention to what was said and what was omitted.

"You keep looking at Desmond," she observed, nodding toward where Des was holding court near the kitchen, telling a story that had four people laughing. "You jealous?"

"Of what?"

"Of the way he does that." She gestured vaguely at the room, at the social machinery operating around us. "The ease of it."

"No," I said, and then, because the jungle juice was making me honest: "Yes. Obviously."

"He's performing, too. Everyone here is. The difference is some people enjoy the performance."

I thought about this. It was the smartest thing anyone had said to me since I arrived, and it came from a girl with a moon tattoo holding a red cup at a party I hadn't wanted to attend.

We were still talking when the night shifted. It happened the way weather changes in Los Angeles -- suddenly, without warning, the sky going from clear to strange. There was a commotion near the front door. Raised voices. A girl crying. Someone said the word "ambulance" and the music stopped with the abruptness of a heart monitor flatlining.

A freshman -- a boy named Tyler from our floor -- had collapsed in the bathroom. Not alcohol poisoning, as it turned out, but a panic attack so severe he'd lost consciousness. The paramedics came anyway. They carried him out, and the party kept going, which was the part that stayed with me -- not the collapse itself, but the machinery grinding forward, the music resuming before the ambulance had even pulled away.

Desmond found me outside. I was sitting on the steps with June.

"You okay?" he asked, and sat down next to us. For the first time, I saw something flicker behind the easy smile -- something heavier, something managed. He pulled out his phone, checked a notification, and his jaw tightened in a way that had nothing to do with Tyler or the party. He put the phone away without responding.

June left around midnight. She gave me her number, written on the back of a receipt from the campus bookstore, which felt deliberately analog, like a choice. I folded it carefully and put it in my pocket, and it stayed there for three days before I texted her, because I was the kind of person who rehearsed even text messages.

Walking back to Hedrick in the dark, Desmond and I were quiet for a long time. The campus was beautiful at night -- the buildings lit from below, the trees casting long shadows on the paths, the occasional coyote trotting across a lawn with the indifference of a local who'd seen too many freshmen come and go to be impressed.

"You ever feel like you're faking it?" I asked. The question came out before I could stop it, propelled by jungle juice and the image of Tyler on the stretcher.

Desmond didn't answer for a while. We passed Royce Hall, its towers dark against the sky. A sprinkler was going somewhere, the sound of water hitting grass in the night.

"Every day," he said. "I just fake it louder."

It rearranged something in my understanding of him -- this boy who seemed frictionless, admitting the friction was there all along, just performed so well that even his roommate couldn't see it.

We got back to the room. Desmond climbed into bed without turning on the light. I sat at my desk and opened my laptop and looked at my Flannery O'Connor paper and realized I had nothing left to say about revelation and grace. I closed the laptop.

Instead, I opened a new document and wrote down everything that had happened that night -- the party, June's laugh, Tyler's collapse, Desmond's confession on the walk home. I didn't know why I was writing it. I didn't know who it was for. I just knew that the day had contained more life than I was equipped to process, and writing was the only technology I had for slowing it down.

I fell asleep at the desk. In the morning, Desmond was already gone. My neck ached. My mouth tasted like bad decisions. On my laptop screen, the cursor blinked at the end of the last sentence I'd written before sleep took me:

"I think I might be someone here. I just don't know who yet."

A notification was waiting in my inbox -- from the Daily Bruin, the campus newspaper. Open submissions for personal essays. Five hundred words on "the first time you felt seen." I read the prompt three times. Then I looked at what I'd written the night before, and for the first time since arriving at UCLA, I thought: maybe this is what I'm for.
