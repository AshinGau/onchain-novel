# Rules: Freshman Year at UCLA

## Setting
Present-day Los Angeles, UCLA campus, September through November of freshman year. Key locations include: Hedrick Hall Room 417 (the dorm room -- cinder-block walls, industrial carpet, two beds six feet apart, a desk with "YOU WILL SURVIVE THIS" scratched into the wood), De Neve dining hall, Bruin Walk (the main campus artery under sycamores), Powell Library (third floor, the windows with afternoon light), Kerckhoff Hall coffee shop (corner table by the window), Royce Hall (Romanesque arches), Janss Steps, Greek Row on Gayley Avenue, Landfair Avenue apartment parties. The campus is beautiful, indifferent, and overwhelming -- ten thousand strangers pretending they know what they are doing. Los Angeles weather is constant, sunny, a lie that promises nothing bad can happen while the ground shifts beneath it. The economic divide between students is ever-present: BMWs in the parking structure next to a first-generation student's used laptop.

## Characters
- **Elias Reyes** (narrator, first person) -- Freshman from Bakersfield, California. First in his family to attend a four-year university. Latino (full name: Elias Reyes-Gutierrez, shortened in fifth grade after a teacher turned his surname into a punchline). Father is a welder with hands "like topographic maps." Mother is loving, performative in public, makes beds with trembling precision. Elias is on a Pell Grant. He is quiet, literary, intensely self-conscious, and prone to hiding in the library. He rehearses text messages before sending them. His default mode is observation and self-doubt, but he has a sharp, dry wit that emerges when he feels safe. He discovers a talent for personal essay writing. His internal conflict: the suffocating awareness that every step on campus is a step no one in his family has taken before, and the freedom of that is indistinguishable from terror. He lies to his mother by text (three lies on the first night). He starts writing as a way to slow life down to a processable speed.

- **Desmond Carter** (full name: Desmond Otieno-Carter) -- Elias's roommate. From Ladera Heights (Black Beverly Hills, LA). Tall (six-two or six-three), lean, warm smile that seems calibrated but genuine. Wears his father's faded Howard University t-shirt. Has a podcast setup (two monitors, ring light, microphone). Socially brilliant -- builds a social architecture within weeks. His ease appears natural but is actually discipline: "I just fake it louder." His father is Henry Otieno, a Kenyan opposition leader whose anti-corruption campaign has made the family targets of political violence. Desmond was sent to UCLA for safety. His father was attacked (car bombed -- broken ribs, punctured lung, survived because the car was armored). Desmond carries this alone, making calls in Swahili at 3 a.m., crying facing the wall. A framed photograph on his shelf is turned face-down (likely family). He chose not to tell anyone about his background, using a security consultant's advice that "nobody pays attention to African politics." His smile is not performance but discipline -- the daily choice to be warm in a world trying to kill his father.

- **June** -- From San Jose. Taiwanese-American. Molecular biology major (which she describes as "a cry for help"). Parents own a boba shop and want her to be a doctor -- "love expressed in the imperative tense." Has a younger brother who is the favorite child, a fact she delivers with cheerful fatalism. Short, with a streak of silver in her black hair and a crescent moon tattoo behind her left ear (she touches it when thinking). Wears vintage band t-shirts. Funny -- genuinely, not defensively. She asks questions "the way good readers read." She found Desmond's secret through a reverse image search while researching Kenyan student movements for a sociology paper. She texts her mother every night in Mandarin -- conversations she never translates. She gave Elias her number on the back of a bookstore receipt (deliberately analog). She and Elias exist in a charged, unspoken space -- clearly moving toward each other but neither has named it.

- **Tyler** -- Freshman on their floor at Hedrick Hall. Collapsed at a party from a panic attack so severe he lost consciousness. Carried out by paramedics. Represents the hidden fragility beneath the performative ease of college life.

- **Elias's father** -- A welder from Bakersfield. Took the day off work (eighty dollars they didn't have) to drive Elias to UCLA. Wears a blue shirt with snap buttons. Shook Elias's hand instead of hugging -- a grip that communicated everything his words couldn't. Said "Make us proud," meaning "don't forget us." Has a drinking problem (mentioned once, briefly).

- **Elias's mother** -- Bright "performing voice" in public. Made Elias's bed at the dorm with trembling hands. Cried in the parking structure. Loves by protecting and by refusing to let Elias see her fear.

## Story Rules
- **Genre**: Literary campus novel about class, identity, belonging, and the weight of being first. Not a coming-of-age romp -- a quiet, interior story about impostor syndrome, friendship, and the things people carry invisibly.
- **Narrative voice**: First person, Elias. His voice is literary, precise, self-aware, and often self-deprecating. He uses long, observational sentences that build through detail. His metaphors are grounded and surprising ("a grip that communicated everything his words couldn't," "smart was the minimum"). He processes the world through writing -- the act of writing is itself a plot element.
- **Pacing**: Measured. Each chapter covers a significant emotional event (move-in day, first party, discovering Desmond's secret) but the real story is in the accumulation of small observations. Time between events is compressed through reflection.
- **Class consciousness**: Ever-present but never preachy. The economic gulf shows through details -- Pell Grant vs. Porsche, black coffee because it's the only drink he knows how to pronounce, the father's eighty-dollar sacrifice. Do not lecture about inequality; let the details speak.
- **Performance as theme**: Everyone is performing. Desmond's ease is discipline. June's casual brilliance is studied. Elias's hiding is a performance of invisibility. The Landfair party scene makes this explicit. The question is not whether people are faking it, but what the performance costs.
- **What to avoid**: Do not make this a triumphant "first-gen success story" with easy resolution. Do not reduce Desmond to his trauma or June to a love interest. Do not make UCLA the enemy -- the institution is indifferent, which is worse than hostile. Avoid melodrama around Desmond's father; the power is in how normally Des tries to live while carrying an extraordinary burden. Do not resolve the Elias-June relationship quickly -- the unspoken tension is more interesting than declaration.

## Plot Direction
- **Elias's writing**: He has discovered personal essay writing and seen the Daily Bruin's open submission call ("the first time you felt seen"). This is a major thread -- will he submit? What will he write? Writing may become his identity at UCLA, the answer to "maybe this is what I'm for."
- **Desmond's father**: Henry Otieno survived the attack but the situation is ongoing. Desmond now has two people who know his secret (Elias and June). How does this change his daily performance? Will there be further threats? Will he need to leave UCLA?
- **Elias and June**: They are clearly moving toward each other but have agreed not to name it. Their relationship is built on intellectual honesty and mutual recognition of outsider status. The next step is uncertain -- will one of them name it?
- **Desmond and Elias's friendship**: The roommate relationship has transformed from strangers to something deeper -- "two people who had seen each other without the costume and chose to keep walking." This friendship is the emotional backbone of the story.
- **Elias's family**: His father's drinking is mentioned once and not explored. His parents' sacrifice and expectations are a constant undercurrent. How does Elias reconcile his new world with theirs?
- **The face-down photograph**: On Desmond's shelf. Never explained. What is it?
- **Tyler's collapse**: Represents the broader crisis of freshman mental health. Could resurface as a theme or plot point.
- **The desk inscription**: "YOU WILL SURVIVE THIS" -- a message from a previous occupant. This could become meaningful if Elias leaves his own mark.
