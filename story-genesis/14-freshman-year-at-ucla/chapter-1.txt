Bootstrap Chapter 1: The Weight of Small Things

The first thing I lost at UCLA was my voice. Not metaphorically -- I mean I opened my mouth to say hello to my new roommate and nothing came out. Just a dry click at the back of my throat, like a lock turning in an empty room.

He was already there when I arrived. That was the first surprise. I'd imagined the moment a hundred times during the drive down from Bakersfield -- me arriving first, choosing the bed by the window, arranging my books on the shelf so the spines formed a gradient from dark to light, a private signal of control in a world about to come undone. Instead I walked into Room 417 of Hedrick Hall and found a boy sitting cross-legged on the window bed, laptop open, wearing headphones the size of grapefruit halves, nodding to something I couldn't hear.

My mother was behind me with the box of sheets. My father was behind her with the mini-fridge, breathing hard from the stairs because we couldn't find the elevator. The boy on the bed looked up and smiled. He had the kind of smile that seemed practiced -- not fake, exactly, but calibrated, the way some people learn to make their face a gift to strangers.

"You must be Elias," he said, pulling off the headphones. His voice was easy and warm, the vowels carrying the loose confidence of someone who had never once rehearsed a greeting in the shower. "I'm Desmond. Des. I took the window side -- hope that's cool. I can switch if you want."

I opened my mouth. Click.

"It's fine," my mother said behind me. She had that bright, performing voice she used in public, the one that made me feel simultaneously protected and erased. "Elias is just taking it all in. Aren't you, mijo?"

I nodded.

Desmond stood, and I realized he was tall -- six-two, maybe six-three, with the kind of lean build that suggested sports without screaming it. He wore a faded Howard University t-shirt, which I'd later learn belonged to his father, and basketball shorts, and no shoes. He crossed the room in two strides and shook my father's hand, then my mother's, and then mine, and his palm was dry and his grip was perfect and I hated him a little for all of it.

"Where you from?" he asked.

"Bakersfield," my father said, setting down the mini-fridge with a grunt. I could see him cataloging the room -- the cinder-block walls, the industrial carpet, the window that looked out onto a courtyard where someone was already playing guitar badly. He was doing the math, I knew. Thirty-two thousand dollars a year to live in a room smaller than our garage.

"Oh, cool. I'm from Ladera Heights." Desmond said it without emphasis, but I'd later learn what Ladera Heights meant -- View Park adjacent, Black Beverly Hills, the kind of neighborhood where you grew up knowing senators. He pointed to the desk he'd already set up. Two monitors. A ring light. A microphone on a boom arm. "I do a podcast. Music stuff. Hope the setup's not too much."

I noticed, on the shelf above his desk, a framed photograph turned face-down. The kind of deliberate concealment that looks casual but isn't. I wouldn't think about that photograph again for seven weeks.

It was too much. All of it was too much. The room smelled like industrial cleaner and someone else's cologne, and when I set my box down on the desk I noticed, scratched into the wood in small, deliberate letters: YOU WILL SURVIVE THIS. Some previous occupant's message in a bottle. I ran my thumb over the grooves and felt oddly steadied, as though the room itself had a memory, and the memory was kind. Through the thin wall I could hear a girl laughing -- a cascading, generous laugh that made me feel lonelier than silence would have. I wanted to sit on my bare mattress and put my head in my hands, but my parents were watching me with that look -- hopeful, terrified, proud, the whole contradictory weather system of sending your first-born to a place you'd never been.

My mother made my bed. I let her, even though I was eighteen and she was the reason I'd never learned to do it properly. She tucked the corners with the precision of someone performing a final act, and when she smoothed the pillow I saw her hand tremble, just once, and I looked away because some things between mothers and sons should not be witnessed by strangers.

My father walked to the window and looked out. "Nice campus," he said, which was the most he could manage. He was a welder. He had hands like topographic maps. He'd taken the day off work, which meant eighty dollars we didn't have, and he stood at that window in his good shirt -- the blue one with the snap buttons -- and I understood that he was memorizing the view so he could describe it to my mother's sisters, who would ask about the university with the particular hunger of people who had imagined such places only from the outside.

We went to lunch at De Neve, which is what UCLA calls a dining hall but which felt, to me, like an airport terminal for the young and beautiful. There were students everywhere -- impossibly confident, impossibly at ease, carrying trays with the casual authority of people who had always belonged to institutions like this. A girl walked past with a plate of sushi and a copy of Derrida, carrying both with the nonchalance of someone who had never questioned whether she belonged anywhere.

My mother ordered a salad and didn't eat it. My father ate everything and said it was good, even the parts that weren't, because that was how he loved -- by refusing to acknowledge disappointment. Desmond had disappeared somewhere, giving us space with the social intelligence I was already beginning to resent.

After lunch we walked Bruin Walk, the main artery of campus, a wide pathway shaded by sycamores where club recruiters were already setting up tables, their voices overlapping in a bright, competitive chorus. Sign up for Model UN! Join the Pre-Med Society! Come to Hillel! Come to the Muslim Student Association! Come to the anime club, the debate team, the Quidditch league, the sustainable food collective, the a cappella group that had once opened for John Legend, allegedly. Each table offered a version of who I could become, and the abundance was paralyzing. In Bakersfield I had been the smart kid, the quiet one, the boy who read too much and talked too little, and that identity had been enough because it was the only one on offer. Here, everyone was the smart kid. Here, smart was the minimum.

My parents left at three o'clock. My mother cried in the parking structure, which I expected. My father shook my hand, which I didn't. He held it for a long time, and his grip was different from Desmond's -- rough, uncertain, a grip that communicated everything his words couldn't. "Make us proud," he said, and I heard what he didn't say: don't forget us.

I watched the minivan until it turned onto Hilgard Avenue and disappeared. Then I stood in the parking structure, Level 4, surrounded by BMWs and Teslas and a Porsche with a Greek letter decal, and I cried. Not dramatically. Not with sound. Just tears falling onto concrete in the specific silence of a boy who wanted something so badly that getting it feels like loss.

When I got back to the room, Desmond was there, changed into a fresh shirt, scrolling his phone. He looked up and read my face with an accuracy that startled me.

"First day's the worst," he said. "Gets better. You want to walk around? I know where the good coffee is."

I found my voice. It was small, and it cracked on the second word, but it was there. "Yeah," I said. "Okay."

We walked out into the September light, and the campus opened up before us -- Royce Hall's Romanesque arches glowing amber, Janss Steps cascading down to a lawn where someone was doing yoga and someone else was arguing about Foucault, the whole improbable theater of ten thousand strangers pretending they knew what they were doing. Desmond walked beside me, pointing out buildings, naming things -- Powell Library, Kerckhoff Hall, the sculpture garden where, he said, people hooked up after dark.

"How do you know all this?" I asked.

"My cousin graduated two years ago. Gave me the full download." He grinned. "Cheat codes."

I didn't have cheat codes. I had a Pell Grant, a used laptop, and the suffocating awareness that I was the first person in my family to attend a four-year university. Every step I took on this campus was a step no one I loved had taken before me, and the freedom of that was indistinguishable from the terror.

We got coffee at Kerckhoff. Desmond ordered something with oat milk and a name I didn't recognize. I ordered black coffee and pretended it was a choice rather than the only drink I knew how to pronounce.

That night, in the dark, with Desmond's breathing settling into the slow rhythm of someone who fell asleep easily, I lay on my mother's perfectly made bed and listened to the sounds of the building -- doors opening, laughter spilling down the hallway, music playing two floors up, a girl singing in the shower. Hedrick Hall hummed with the collective restlessness of eight hundred freshmen on their first night, and I felt the way I imagine astronauts feel when they look back at Earth -- connected to something vast and beautiful and utterly beyond my control.

I pulled out my phone and typed a text to my mother: Got here safe. Room is nice. Roommate seems cool.

Three lies was a good start, I thought. College was going to require a lot of them.

Desmond's phone buzzed in the dark. He rolled over, checked it, and I heard him exhale -- a long, controlled breath, the kind you learn when you're managing something bigger than sleep. He typed a short reply, the screen light catching his face for a moment, and in that blue glow he looked older than eighteen, and far from home in a way that had nothing to do with geography.
