Chapter 2: The Name She Would Not Say

The man from the kitchen returned -- not to the house but to the alley, where he sat on a plastic stool beside the motorbike repair shop, smoking a cigarette with the deliberate slowness of someone who has been waiting a long time and is prepared to wait longer.

His name was Uncle Bao, though he was not Minh's uncle. In Vietnamese the word is a courtesy, a way of placing someone in the architecture of obligation without specifying the room. Bao had served with Khanh. This much he offered freely, standing up when Minh approached, his trousers too large for him, as though he had once been a bigger man and had been whittled down by something that was not age.

"Walk with me," Bao said. "Certain conversations require movement."

They walked along the canal in District 4, past the welders whose arcs sent blue light into the dusk, past the temple where old women fed catfish in murky water as acts of merit. The air smelled of rust and frying oil and the particular sweetness of jasmine that grows in neglected places.

"Your father was an intelligence liaison," Bao said. "He worked between the ARVN command and the Americans, translating not just language but intentions. Each side wanted to believe certain things about the other. Your father stood in the middle and decided which beliefs to permit."

He paused to light another cigarette, and Minh noticed his hands -- the fingers were crooked, healed wrong, as though they had been broken and set without medical attention. Bao saw him looking and said nothing about it, which told Minh more than an explanation would have.

"In April 1975, when Saigon was falling, the Americans organized evacuations. Not for everyone. For those useful enough to earn a seat. Your father had a place on a helicopter -- for him, your mother, and you. You were six months old."

Six months. Minh had imagined himself older, a toddler at least, someone who might carry fragments of memory. But at six months he would have been pure biology, knowing nothing of wars or helicopters or the difference between countries.

"A fellow officer -- a man your father trusted, a man he called brother -- told him the evacuation point had changed. A secondary site, near the port. Your father believed this because the man had worked beside him for three years."

Bao stopped on a small bridge over the canal. Below, the water was black, carrying the reflections of streetlights in long distorted streaks. A woman paddled past in a sampan loaded with morning-market vegetables, her oar keeping time like a slow heartbeat.

"The secondary site was empty. No helicopter. No boat. Your father's place on the evacuation had been taken -- by the man who sent him there. Dang Tuan Phong."

The name landed in the air between them. Three syllables. A stranger's name. Bao spoke it with the precision of someone handling something volatile.

"Phong was junior to your father. Less connected, less valuable. But he understood something about collapsing systems -- that loyalty is a currency that can be counterfeited. He told the Americans your father was compromised, feeding information to the North. A lie, but in April 1975, no one verified anything. They struck your father's name. Phong took his seat. Phong flew out."

"And my father stayed," Minh said.

"Your father tried to reach the port with your mother and you. The crowds were -- you have seen the photographs. Your mother was separated from him. She handed you over a fence to a woman she did not know."

"Ba Tam."

"Ba Tam. Who was also trying to reach the ships. Your mother went back to find your father. She did not find him."

Bao's voice had gone flat, the way people speak when they have rehearsed words so many times the inflection has worn away.

"Your father was arrested two days after the fall. Re-education camp Z30, near Xuan Loc. Seven years." Bao looked at his own crooked hands, the fingers bent at angles that had nothing to do with arthritis. "I was in the same camp. Different section. We broke rocks for road gravel, twelve hours, in sun that peeled the skin off your shoulders in sheets. We could not speak to each other, but I saw him once, through a fence. He was carrying a boulder with both arms, and his legs were shaking so badly I thought he would collapse. I did not recognize him. Someone had to tell me it was Khanh. He had gone into the camp weighing seventy kilograms. He came out at forty-three."

Minh gripped the bridge railing. The metal was warm, rough with rust, its texture printing into his palms.

"He came back to Saigon," Bao continued. "Found Ba Tam. Found you. You were seven. You did not know him. Ba Tam told you he was a friend from the countryside. You do not remember because there was nothing to remember -- a thin man in the kitchen, drinking tea, watching you do homework with an intensity that would have been frightening if you had been old enough to notice."

"I remember a man," Minh said slowly. The memory surfaced like something long submerged -- not a face but a sensation: the smell of eucalyptus oil, hands that trembled around a teacup, a voice that spoke to him with excessive formality, as though addressing someone important. "He brought me a carved wooden bird."

Bao closed his eyes briefly. "He made that in the camp. Carved it from a piece of roof beam with a nail. He kept it for five years before he could give it to you."

The carved bird. Minh still had it, in a box under his sleeping mat. He had always assumed Ba Tam had bought it at a market. A prison carving, held for five years. The object re-formed itself in his mind, became something else entirely -- not a toy but a message, sent across years of silence from a father to a son who did not know he was a son.

"In 1985, your father tried to leave by boat. He wanted to reach America. Not to escape -- he had made a kind of peace with Vietnam. He wanted to find Phong." Bao's voice dropped. "The boat sank off Vung Tau. Forty-three people. Seven survived. Your father was not among them."

The canal smell pressed in -- rot and diesel and the green tang of water hyacinth. A television played a game show somewhere in the houses along the bank.

"My mother?" Minh asked.

"Nguyen Thi Lan. She was in a camp as well, in Long Khanh province. The official record says malaria, 1976. The unofficial record --" Bao paused. "There is no unofficial record. That is the point. Records from that time are incomplete by design."

Minh opened his eyes to the canal, the bridge, the streetlights, the city that was Saigon and Ho Chi Minh City and somehow both at once.

"You said my father wanted to find Phong. To do what?"

"He never used the word revenge. He said he wanted to ask Phong one question: whether the seat on the helicopter had been worth it. Whether Phong's life in America -- Orange County, some kind of import business -- tasted different knowing how it was obtained."

"That is not a question," Minh said. "That is a verdict delivered as a question."

Bao studied him with an expression Minh could not entirely read -- pride, perhaps, but threaded with something else. A hunger. The look of a man who has carried a burden for decades and is finally handing it to someone young enough and angry enough to do something with it. "You are very much your father's son. And that is what frightens Ba Tam."

They stood on the bridge. The city moved around them -- honking, food smoke, heat that would not relent, ten thousand negotiations between people and machines and weather that constituted a Saigon evening.

"There is something else," Bao said. He reached into his shirt pocket and produced a folded paper, soft with age, its creases nearly worn through. "Your father gave me this before he got on the boat. In case he did not come back."

Minh unfolded it. A list, written in his father's small precise hand. Names, dates, locations. At the top: a Saigon address he did not recognize. Below it: two names besides Phong's -- people who had witnessed the betrayal, or participated in it, or profited from it. At the bottom, a single line in English, as though Khanh had been practicing the language he would need: "The debt is not mine alone."

"I do not know what that means," Bao said. But he said it too quickly, Minh thought, the way people deny knowledge they have already organized into a shape. "I think it means that what happened to your family was not one man's decision. Phong pulled the trigger, but someone loaded the gun."

Minh refolded the paper and put it in his pocket, next to the notebook where he had once written MACV and waited years for the meaning.

He walked home through the alley. Ba Tam was asleep, or performing sleep. The shrine was dark, the incense ash. Minh stood before his father's photograph and saw what he now understood -- two men, one smiling, one calculating. A year before the fall. A year before the word "brother" became a lie.

He did not sleep. He lay on his mat and assembled Bao's story into something he could carry. Not a narrative -- narratives have resolution. A map. Incomplete, with blank spaces where the edges dissolved into unknown territory, but a map with a starting point, a list of names, and somewhere in the blankness, a destination.

Under his mat, the carved wooden bird lay in its box. A message from a dead man. Minh reached down and touched it in the dark, feeling the rough grooves where a nail had shaped wood into the form of something that could fly.
