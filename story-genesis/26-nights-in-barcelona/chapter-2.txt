Bootstrap Chapter 2: Wet Paint

They painted each other for three weeks before they touched again.

It began on that Tuesday, in the Picasso Museum courtyard, where Marta arrived carrying a canvas bag heavy with supplies and wearing paint-stained jeans and a black tank top that showed the architecture of her collarbones. She led him not to the galleries but to a bench in the courtyard's corner, where the afternoon light fell in a clean diagonal across the flagstones.

"Draw me," she said. "Not my face. My hands."

So he drew her hands while she drew his, facing each other on the stone bench, sketchbooks on their knees, silent for two hours. Her hands were long-fingered, scarred with small burns from turpentine and hot wax, the nails stained cadmium yellow. He drew them with devotional concentration, and when he showed her the result she studied it a long time before nodding.

"You see too much," she said. "That's your problem. You see everything and you judge it before it reaches the page."

"And your problem?"

"I don't see enough. I feel it and put it down before I understand what it is." She held up her sketch of his hands. They looked like someone else's hands -- rougher, more capable, the hands of a man who built things rather than a man who represented them. "See? I've made you into who I want you to be. That's a kind of lie."

"All art is a kind of lie."

"Yes. But some lies are more useful than others."

They fell into a rhythm. Mornings apart -- Leo in his Eixample studio, Marta in her Raval cave -- and afternoons together, drawing in public spaces across the city. The Cathedral cloisters, where thirteen white geese patrolled the garden. Barceloneta beach, barefoot on warm sand. The Boqueria, where she haggled with a fruit vendor over figs, her Andalusian accent thickening as the price dropped, while he drew her hands in motion.

Each session brought them closer without arriving. He drew her shoulders, the small of her back where her shirt rode up. She drew his jaw, his wrists, the hollow at the base of his throat she called la fossa. They were mapping each other from the periphery inward, and the incompleteness generated a tension Leo felt in his teeth, his fingertips, the involuntary tightening of his abdomen when she leaned close and her breath touched his neck.

"You're torturing me," he told her one evening. They were on the rooftop of her building, sitting on overturned paint buckets, watching the sun drop behind Tibidabo. Below them the Cerdà grid of the Eixample stretched to the sea, and the Sagrada Familia's crane-topped spires rose in the middle distance -- Gaudí's unfinished cathedral, perpetually becoming, the city's patron saint of incompletion. The air smelled of sardines grilling on the restaurant terrace below and the hot tar of the rooftop softening in the last heat of the day.

"I'm teaching you patience." She turned to face him, and in the last red light her eyes held something that might have been fear. "You want to skip to the end. But the end is where things die."

He kissed her. Or she kissed him -- the distinction dissolved in the act, the way a wave dissolves the line between sea and shore. Her mouth tasted of the Garnacha they'd been passing back and forth, and her hand came up to the back of his neck with a grip that had anger in it, or hunger -- some force that went past tenderness into a place where tenderness was too slow, too careful, too much like the restraint they had both been performing for three weeks.

When they pulled apart, she was breathing hard, and so was he.

"Come inside," she said.

Her studio at night was a different country. She lit dozens of cheap votives instead of the overhead light, and in the flickering glow the canvases on the walls seemed to breathe. She set up two easels facing each other, close enough to reach across the gap, and placed a mirror to their right.

"We paint," she said. "At the same time. Each other. However it happens."

She pulled her tank top over her head with the practical efficiency of someone who has posed and been posed a thousand times. No coyness, no performance -- just a woman removing a barrier to visibility. Her body was lean and brown, small-breasted, muscular in the arms and shoulders from years of working at scale. The scar continued from her jaw down her neck and across her left shoulder, a pale meridian that divided her skin into hemispheres.

Leo picked up his brush. His hand was steady in a way it hadn't been for months. He began with the scar -- always the scar, his point of entry into the landscape of her -- and built outward, mixing skin tones on his palette while she watched him from behind her own canvas, her brush moving in quick, confident strokes.

Painting someone bare while they paint you is an intimacy without precedent. Each brushstroke becomes a surrogate touch, each color a confession. He found himself painting not what he saw but what he wanted: the hollow beneath her ribs, the curve of her hip where it met the waistband of her jeans -- she had kept them on, and the contrast between skin and denim became the painting's central tension.

"Stop thinking," she said, without looking up from her canvas.

"I'm not thinking."

"You are. I can hear it in your brushstrokes. They sound like someone doing math." She stepped out from behind her easel and crossed the gap between them. She took the brush from his hand, dipped it in the cadmium red on his palette, and drew a line across his forearm, wrist to elbow. The paint was cool and wet, and the brush's bristles left a trail of sensation on his skin that did not stop when the stroke ended.

"Feel that?" she said. "That's what a brushstroke should feel like. Not in your hand. In the body underneath."

She painted his arms, his shoulders, his chest after he pulled off his shirt. Not an image -- a sensation, translated into color. Red for the heat in his sternum. Blue for the tension in his jaw. A streak of yellow along his collarbone that she said was "the place where you hold your breath."

He painted her back, following the scar with titanium white, then filling the space around it with the burnt umber of her skin. She shivered when the brush crossed her spine, and the shiver traveled through the brush handle into his hand and up his arm, and he understood then what she meant about the brushstroke living in the body underneath.

They painted each other for an hour, and by the end the canvases were abandoned -- color mapped onto living skin, their bodies becoming both the medium and the work. When he finally set down the brush and touched her with bare hands, the transition from bristle to skin felt like a change in grammar, from the indicative to the imperative.

What followed was not separate from the painting. It was the painting's completion -- the thing the colors had been reaching toward, the image that could only be made with the full body and not merely the hand. They moved from the easels to the floor, paint transferring between them in the press and slide of skin against skin, cadmium red mixing with burnt umber mixing with the blue she'd used on his jaw, and when he traced the scar with his tongue from her shoulder to her jawline she tasted of salt and turpentine and the mineral tang of pigment.

She was not gentle, and neither was he. There was something combative in it -- a contest of attention, each trying to see the other more completely, to get beneath surface into substrate. She pulled him into her with a sound that was the inverse of a gasp, a sharp intake of silence, and the candles flickered as though a door had opened somewhere, and all of Barcelona compressed into the space between their bodies.

Afterward, they lay on the paint-smeared floor. The canvases on the easels were wild, streaked with color like weather maps of unknown countries. Their bodies were tattooed with each other's palette, red and blue and yellow drying on skin still damp with sweat. On the ceiling, the candlelight threw their combined shadow into a single trembling shape.

"Now you can paint," Marta said. "You just had to remember that painting is a physical act. Not a mental one."

"What are we doing?" he asked.

"We're making something. Don't name it yet." She rolled onto her side, facing him. "When you name a thing, you give it boundaries. And I don't want boundaries. Not with you, not with this."

He wanted to argue. He was a man who named things -- it was what painters did, what language did, what the whole Western tradition of representation was built on. But the paint was drying on his skin in patterns he had not chosen, and her hand was on his chest again -- that same diagnostic palm, reading his heartbeat -- and he understood that she was offering him something rarer than sex or even love: the chance to exist, for a while, in the space before the name, where things were still becoming.

In the morning, he walked home through the waking Raval. A shopkeeper hosing the sidewalk. The hiss of an espresso machine through an open door. A woman hanging laundry between balconies, wet sheets catching the first sun. Near the MACBA, a lone skateboarder carved arcs across the white plaza. Leo carried the dried paint on his arms like a map of the night, and when he reached his studio he went straight to his largest canvas and began to paint with a fury he had forgotten he possessed.

The painting he made that morning was the best work of his life. He knew it in his body, not his mind -- two figures dissolving into each other, not romantically but elementally, the way water dissolves into water. The colors were the colors of the night before: cadmium red, burnt umber, the blue of a jaw held tight against feeling.

He photographed it and sent it to her. She replied with one word: "More."
